Unit 5: Period 5

1844 – 1877  |  Exam Weight: 10–17%

🇺🇸 Unit Overview

APUSH Unit 5 covers one of the most important turning points in United States history. During this period, the nation expanded west, fought Mexico, gained huge new territories, and then became more divided over slavery. Political compromises failed, sectional tension increased, Southern states seceded, and the Civil War decided the future of slavery and the Union. Reconstruction then tried to rebuild the South and define freedom for formerly enslaved people, but it faced violent resistance and eventually ended without full equality.

Big question: How did westward expansion, slavery, sectional conflict, the Civil War, and Reconstruction transform the United States between 1844 and 1877?

Topic 5.1 — Contextualizing Period 5

🌎 Expansion, Slavery, War, and Reconstruction

Period 5 focuses on the growing conflict between national expansion and slavery. The United States gained enormous new territories, but every new territory raised the question: would slavery expand west?

Major Themes

  • Manifest Destiny: Many Americans believed the U.S. was destined to expand across the continent.
  • Sectionalism: The North and South developed different economies, labor systems, and political goals.
  • Slavery: The central conflict of the period was whether slavery would expand, continue, or end.
  • Civil War: The war preserved the Union and destroyed slavery.
  • Reconstruction: The federal government tried to rebuild the South and define citizenship after slavery.

Topic 5.2 — Manifest Destiny and Westward Expansion

🧭 Expansion Across the Continent

Manifest Destiny was the belief that the United States was meant to expand across North America. Supporters saw expansion as spreading democracy and opportunity, while critics argued it promoted war, Native removal, and slavery.

Major Examples of Expansion

  • Texas annexation: Texas joined the United States in 1845 after previously winning independence from Mexico.
  • Oregon Territory: The U.S. and Britain agreed to divide Oregon at the 49th parallel in 1846.
  • Mexican Cession: The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo gave the U.S. California and much of the Southwest.
  • California Gold Rush: The discovery of gold in 1848 brought thousands of migrants west.

Effects of Expansion

  • Expansion increased conflict with Mexico and Native peoples.
  • New western lands intensified debate over whether slavery should spread.
  • Migration changed the environment and disrupted Native communities.
  • The U.S. became a continental power stretching from the Atlantic to the Pacific.

Topic 5.3 — The Mexican-American War

⚔️ War With Mexico

The Mexican-American War was strongly connected to Manifest Destiny. President James K. Polk wanted to acquire California and New Mexico, while Mexico disputed the U.S. annexation of Texas and the border.

Causes

  • Texas annexation: Mexico still considered Texas part of its territory.
  • Border dispute: The U.S. claimed the Rio Grande as Texas's border, while Mexico claimed the Nueces River.
  • Polk's expansionism: Polk wanted California, New Mexico, and other western lands.

Results

  • Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo: Mexico ceded California and much of the Southwest to the U.S.
  • Mexican Cession: New land created intense debate over slavery in the territories.
  • Wilmot Proviso: Proposed banning slavery in land taken from Mexico, but it failed in the Senate.
  • Sectional conflict: Many Northerners saw the war as an attempt to expand slave territory.

Topic 5.4 — The Compromise of 1850 and Sectional Conflict

⚖️ Compromise Begins to Fail

After the Mexican-American War, the country struggled to decide whether slavery would be allowed in new western territories. The Compromise of 1850 temporarily reduced tension but also made conflict worse in the long run.

Compromise of 1850

  • California: Admitted as a free state.
  • Popular sovereignty: Utah and New Mexico territories would decide slavery by vote.
  • Slave trade: The slave trade ended in Washington, D.C., but slavery itself continued there.
  • Fugitive Slave Law: Required Northerners to help return escaped enslaved people and punished those who helped them.

Effects

  • The Fugitive Slave Law angered many Northerners and increased abolitionist activity.
  • Southern slaveholders demanded stronger protection for slavery.
  • Compromise became harder as both sides became more extreme.

Topic 5.5 — Slavery, Abolition, and Resistance

⛓️ Slavery and the Fight Against It

Slavery remained the most divisive issue in the United States. Enslaved people resisted slavery, abolitionists attacked slavery morally and politically, and Southern defenders argued slavery was necessary and positive.

Enslaved Resistance

  • Everyday resistance: Enslaved people slowed work, broke tools, preserved culture, and resisted control.
  • Escape: Some enslaved people escaped through the Underground Railroad.
  • Rebellion: Revolts like Nat Turner's Rebellion increased Southern fear and repression.

Abolitionism

  • William Lloyd Garrison: Published The Liberator and demanded immediate abolition.
  • Frederick Douglass: Formerly enslaved abolitionist who used speeches and writing to expose slavery's brutality.
  • Harriet Tubman: Helped enslaved people escape through the Underground Railroad.
  • Uncle Tom's Cabin: Harriet Beecher Stowe's novel increased Northern opposition to slavery.

Southern Defense of Slavery

  • Southern leaders argued slavery was protected by property rights and the Constitution.
  • Some claimed slavery was a “positive good,” not just a necessary evil.
  • Southern states passed stricter laws to control enslaved people and silence abolitionist ideas.
Frederick Douglass — connects to abolitionism, antislavery activism, and Black resistance to slavery.

Topic 5.6 — The 1850s: The Road to Civil War

🔥 Sectional Crisis

During the 1850s, the political system failed to contain conflict over slavery. Violence spread, parties collapsed, and the Republican Party rose by opposing the expansion of slavery.

Kansas-Nebraska Act

  • Popular sovereignty: Allowed settlers in Kansas and Nebraska to vote on slavery.
  • Repealed Missouri Compromise line: Opened land north of 36°30′ to possible slavery.
  • Bleeding Kansas: Proslavery and antislavery settlers fought violently over Kansas's future.

Dred Scott Decision

  • Supreme Court ruling: Dred Scott was not a citizen and could not sue in federal court.
  • Territories: Congress could not ban slavery in the territories.
  • Impact: The decision angered Northerners and strengthened the Republican Party.

John Brown and Harpers Ferry

  • Goal: John Brown tried to start a slave uprising by raiding a federal arsenal.
  • Southern reaction: Many Southerners saw Brown as proof that abolitionists wanted violent rebellion.
  • Northern reaction: Some Northerners condemned his violence, while others admired his antislavery commitment.

Election of 1860

  • Lincoln: Won as a Republican opposing the expansion of slavery.
  • Southern secession: Southern states believed Lincoln threatened slavery and began leaving the Union.
  • Confederacy: Seceding states formed the Confederate States of America.

Topic 5.7 — The Civil War

🪖 Union vs. Confederacy

The Civil War began after Southern secession and the attack on Fort Sumter. The war started as a fight to preserve the Union, but it became a war to end slavery as well.

Advantages

  • Union advantages: Larger population, more factories, more railroads, stronger navy, and more financial resources.
  • Confederate advantages: Strong military leadership, defensive war, and motivation to protect home territory.

Major Events

  • Fort Sumter: Confederate attack started the Civil War.
  • Antietam: Union victory allowed Lincoln to issue the Emancipation Proclamation.
  • Emancipation Proclamation: Freed enslaved people in rebelling states and made abolition a Union war goal.
  • Gettysburg: Major Union victory that stopped Lee's invasion of the North.
  • Vicksburg: Union victory gave control of the Mississippi River.
  • Appomattox: Lee surrendered to Grant in 1865.

Effects

  • The Union was preserved.
  • Slavery was abolished through the 13th Amendment.
  • Federal power increased.
  • The South was physically and economically devastated.
Emancipation Proclamation — connects to the changing purpose of the Civil War and the fight to end slavery.

Topic 5.8 — Reconstruction

🏛️ Rebuilding the Nation

Reconstruction was the period after the Civil War when the federal government tried to rebuild the South, bring former Confederate states back into the Union, and define the legal status of formerly enslaved people.

Presidential Reconstruction

  • Lincoln's plan: Wanted a relatively quick and lenient reunification.
  • Andrew Johnson: Allowed many former Confederates to regain power and did little to protect freedpeople.
  • Black Codes: Southern laws restricted the freedom and labor rights of formerly enslaved people.

Radical Reconstruction

  • Radical Republicans: Wanted stronger federal protection for freedpeople and punishment of former Confederates.
  • Military Reconstruction Acts: Divided the South into military districts.
  • Freedmen's Bureau: Helped formerly enslaved people with education, labor contracts, food, and legal support.

Reconstruction Amendments

  • 13th Amendment: Abolished slavery.
  • 14th Amendment: Defined citizenship and guaranteed equal protection under the law.
  • 15th Amendment: Protected voting rights for Black men.

Resistance and Failure

  • Ku Klux Klan: Used terror and violence to attack Black political participation and white Republicans.
  • Sharecropping: Kept many Black and poor white farmers in debt and poverty.
  • Compromise of 1877: Ended Reconstruction when federal troops left the South.
  • Legacy: Reconstruction created constitutional protections, but many promises of equality were undermined by white Southern resistance.
Freedmen's Bureau — connects to Reconstruction efforts to support formerly enslaved people.

Cross-Regional Continuities & Change

  • Continuity: Debates over federal power, states' rights, expansion, and race continued throughout the period.
  • Change: The Civil War ended slavery and greatly increased federal authority.
  • Similarities: Both North and South claimed to defend liberty, but they defined liberty very differently.
  • Differences: The North's free labor economy conflicted with the South's slave labor plantation economy.

📝 Multiple Choice Practice

Click an answer to check it. Each question includes an explanation.

1. Which issue most directly intensified after the Mexican-American War?

(A) Whether the United States should remain neutral in Europe.
(B) Whether slavery should be allowed in newly acquired western territories.
(C) Whether the United States should give California back to Mexico.
(D) Whether the Articles of Confederation should be restored.
Answer: B. The Mexican Cession reopened the national debate over whether slavery would expand into western territories, increasing sectional conflict.

2. Which part of the Compromise of 1850 most angered many Northerners?

(A) California entered as a free state.
(B) The slave trade ended in Washington, D.C.
(C) The Fugitive Slave Law required help returning escaped enslaved people.
(D) Texas gave up land claims in exchange for money.
Answer: C. The Fugitive Slave Law forced Northerners to participate in returning escaped enslaved people, which increased antislavery feeling in the North.

3. The Kansas-Nebraska Act contributed to sectional violence mainly because it did which of the following?

(A) Banned slavery in all western territories.
(B) Allowed popular sovereignty and reopened the question of slavery north of the Missouri Compromise line.
(C) Required all enslaved people to be freed immediately.
(D) Ended the Republican Party.
Answer: B. The act allowed settlers to vote on slavery in Kansas and Nebraska, leading proslavery and antislavery settlers to fight in “Bleeding Kansas.”

4. Which statement best describes the significance of the Emancipation Proclamation?

(A) It immediately freed all enslaved people in every state.
(B) It made ending slavery a Union war goal and applied to enslaved people in rebelling states.
(C) It allowed the Confederacy to rejoin the Union without conditions.
(D) It ended Reconstruction.
Answer: B. The Emancipation Proclamation did not free every enslaved person immediately, but it changed the purpose of the Civil War by making abolition a Union goal.

5. Which of the following was a major goal of Radical Reconstruction?

(A) Restore slavery in the South.
(B) Remove all federal authority from the South immediately.
(C) Protect the rights of freedpeople and reshape Southern governments.
(D) Give former Confederate leaders complete control of Reconstruction.
Answer: C. Radical Republicans wanted stronger federal action to protect freedpeople, punish former Confederates, and rebuild Southern governments on new terms.

⭐ Key Takeaways

  • Manifest Destiny expanded the nation. Texas annexation, Oregon, the Mexican Cession, and the Gold Rush pushed the U.S. westward.
  • Expansion made slavery harder to compromise over. Every new territory raised the question of free or slave labor.
  • The 1850s broke the political system. The Compromise of 1850, Kansas-Nebraska Act, Dred Scott, and John Brown deepened sectional division.
  • The Civil War preserved the Union and ended slavery. The war expanded federal power and led to emancipation.
  • Reconstruction expanded rights on paper. The 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments transformed the Constitution.
  • Reconstruction faced violent resistance. Black Codes, the KKK, sharecropping, and the Compromise of 1877 limited freedom and equality.
