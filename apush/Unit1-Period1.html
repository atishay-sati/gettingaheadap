Unit 1: Period 1

1491 – 1607  |  Exam Weight: 4–6%

🇺🇸 Unit Overview

APUSH Unit 1 covers the Americas before permanent English settlement and explains how Native American societies, European exploration, the Columbian Exchange, Spanish colonization, and African labor systems shaped early American history. Before Europeans arrived, North America was filled with diverse Native societies that adapted to different environments. After 1492, contact between Europe, Africa, and the Americas created massive biological, economic, cultural, and social change.

Big question: How did Native American societies develop before European arrival, and how did contact among Native Americans, Europeans, and Africans transform the Atlantic world between 1491 and 1607?

🗓️ Unit 1 Timeline

Before 1492: Diverse Native American societies develop across North America, shaped by geography, climate, agriculture, hunting, fishing, and trade.
c. 1000–1500: Mississippian societies, including Cahokia, build large mound cities and rely heavily on maize agriculture.
1491: The Americas are home to millions of Indigenous people with complex societies, political systems, and trade networks.
1492: Christopher Columbus reaches the Caribbean, beginning sustained contact between Europe, Africa, and the Americas.
1494: Treaty of Tordesillas divides Spanish and Portuguese claims in the Atlantic world.
1512–1513: Spanish Laws of Burgos attempt to regulate treatment of Native peoples but still support forced labor.
1519–1521: Hernán Cortés conquers the Aztec Empire with help from Native allies, disease, weapons, and political divisions.
1530s: Francisco Pizarro conquers the Inca Empire in South America.
1542: New Laws try to limit the encomienda system after criticism from reformers like Bartolomé de Las Casas.
1565: Spain establishes St. Augustine in Florida, the oldest permanent European settlement in what became the United States.
1580s: English attempts at Roanoke show early English interest in North American colonization.
1607: Jamestown is founded in Virginia, marking the start of permanent English settlement in North America.

Topic 1.1 — Contextualizing Period 1

🌽 Native America Before European Contact

Before Europeans arrived, Native American societies were not all the same. They developed different ways of life based on geography, climate, available resources, and agriculture.

Major Patterns

  • Diversity: Native societies had different languages, religions, governments, economies, and social structures.
  • Environmental adaptation: Native groups adapted to their regions through farming, hunting, fishing, gathering, and trade.
  • Maize agriculture: Corn spread from Mesoamerica into North America and supported larger, more settled societies.
  • Trade networks: Native communities exchanged food, tools, shells, copper, and ideas across long distances.

Why This Matters

  • European colonizers entered a world that was already populated, organized, and connected.
  • Native peoples shaped colonial history through resistance, diplomacy, trade, and alliance-building.
  • APUSH often asks you to compare Native societies by region and explain how environment shaped development.

Topic 1.2 — Native American Societies Before European Contact

🏜️ Regional Native Societies

Native American societies developed differently depending on their environments. Some groups built large agricultural settlements, while others remained more mobile because hunting and gathering fit their region better.

Southwest

  • Pueblo peoples: Built adobe and stone villages in the dry Southwest.
  • Irrigation: Used canals and water-control systems to grow maize, beans, and squash in arid areas.
  • Settled communities: Agriculture allowed more permanent towns and complex social organization.

Great Plains

  • Before horses: Many Plains groups combined hunting, gathering, and some farming near rivers.
  • Mobility: Societies were often less permanently settled because the environment favored movement and hunting.
  • After European contact: Horses later transformed Plains life, but that becomes more important in later periods.

Mississippi River Valley / Southeast

  • Mississippian culture: Built large mound cities such as Cahokia.
  • Maize-based agriculture: Supported dense populations and complex chiefdoms.
  • Social hierarchy: Leaders controlled labor, trade, ceremonies, and food distribution.

Northeast / Atlantic Seaboard

  • Mixed economy: Native peoples farmed maize, beans, and squash while also hunting, fishing, and gathering.
  • Woodland societies: Used forests and rivers for transportation, food, and materials.
  • Political organization: Some groups formed alliances or confederacies, such as the Haudenosaunee/Iroquois Confederacy.

Pacific Northwest

  • Fishing societies: Salmon and other marine resources supported permanent villages.
  • Wealth and status: Some groups developed social ranking because food resources were abundant.
  • Trade: Coastal and river networks connected communities across the region.

Topic 1.3 — European Exploration in the Americas

⛵ Europe Looks West

European exploration was driven by economic, religious, and political motives. Europeans wanted wealth, trade routes, converts, and national power.

Causes of Exploration

  • God: Europeans wanted to spread Christianity, especially Catholicism.
  • Gold: Europeans searched for precious metals, trade routes, and profitable goods.
  • Glory: Monarchs wanted power, territory, and competition with rival nations.
  • Technology: The caravel, compass, astrolabe, and better maps helped ocean travel.

Spain and Portugal

  • Portugal: Focused heavily on African and Indian Ocean trade routes.
  • Spain: Sponsored Columbus and quickly built a large empire in the Americas.
  • Treaty of Tordesillas: Divided Spanish and Portuguese claims, giving Spain most of the Americas and Portugal Brazil.

Effects of Exploration

  • Europeans claimed land already inhabited by Native peoples.
  • Competition among European powers increased.
  • The Atlantic Ocean became a major center of trade, empire, migration, and forced labor.

Topic 1.4 — Columbian Exchange, Spanish Exploration, and Conquest

🌎 The Columbian Exchange

The Columbian Exchange was the transfer of plants, animals, diseases, people, and ideas between the Eastern and Western Hemispheres after 1492. It completely changed life in the Americas, Europe, and Africa.

From the Americas to Europe, Africa, and Asia

  • Foods: Maize, potatoes, tomatoes, cacao, peppers, beans, squash, and tobacco spread globally.
  • Population growth: Crops like potatoes and maize helped increase food supplies in Europe, Africa, and Asia.
  • Economic change: American silver and cash crops helped expand global trade.

From Europe, Africa, and Asia to the Americas

  • Animals: Horses, cattle, pigs, and sheep transformed Native life and colonial economies.
  • Crops: Wheat, sugar, rice, and coffee became important in colonial agriculture.
  • Disease: Smallpox, measles, and influenza devastated Native populations with little immunity.

Spanish Conquest

  • Cortés and the Aztecs: Spanish victory was helped by disease, steel weapons, horses, Native allies, and divisions within the Aztec Empire.
  • Pizarro and the Inca: The Spanish used political conflict, disease, and military advantages to conquer the Inca Empire.
  • Native allies: Many Indigenous groups allied with Europeans because they opposed powerful empires like the Aztecs.

Topic 1.5 — Labor, Slavery, and Caste in the Spanish Colonial System

🏰 Spanish Colonization

Spain created a colonial system based on land, Catholic conversion, forced labor, racial hierarchy, and extraction of wealth. This system became one of the first major European colonial models in the Americas.

Encomienda System

  • Definition: Spanish colonists received the right to demand labor and tribute from Native peoples.
  • Justification: Colonists were supposed to protect and Christianize Native peoples.
  • Reality: The system often became brutal forced labor and caused massive suffering.

Catholic Missions

  • Goal: Convert Native peoples to Christianity and spread Spanish culture.
  • Mission system: Priests created missions that combined religion, labor, and colonial control.
  • Native response: Native peoples sometimes converted, blended beliefs, resisted, or fled.

Debate Over Native Treatment

  • Bartolomé de Las Casas: Spanish priest who criticized the abuse of Native peoples and argued they should be treated more humanely.
  • Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda: Argued that Spanish conquest was justified and that Native peoples were inferior.
  • New Laws of 1542: Tried to limit abuses in the encomienda system, but colonists often resisted enforcement.

Casta System

  • Peninsulares: People born in Spain, usually at the top of colonial society.
  • Creoles: People of Spanish descent born in the Americas.
  • Mestizos: People of mixed Spanish and Native ancestry.
  • Mulattoes: People of mixed European and African ancestry.
  • Native Americans and enslaved Africans: Usually placed at the bottom of the social hierarchy.

Topic 1.6 — African Labor and the Atlantic World

⛓️ African Slavery and Atlantic Labor Systems

As Native populations declined because of disease, warfare, and forced labor, Europeans increasingly relied on enslaved Africans to work in colonial economies.

Why African Slavery Expanded

  • Native population decline: Disease and exploitation killed millions of Indigenous people.
  • Plantation labor demand: Sugar plantations required large numbers of workers.
  • European profit: Slavery became tied to Atlantic trade and colonial wealth.
  • Existing trade networks: Europeans expanded and transformed older African slave-trading systems into a larger Atlantic slave trade.

Atlantic Economy

  • Triangular trade: Goods, enslaved people, and raw materials moved among Europe, Africa, and the Americas.
  • Middle Passage: The forced journey of enslaved Africans across the Atlantic under brutal conditions.
  • Sugar colonies: Caribbean and Brazilian plantations became major centers of enslaved labor.

Long-Term Significance

  • African labor became central to the development of the Atlantic economy.
  • Racial slavery created long-lasting social hierarchies in the Americas.
  • Africans resisted slavery through rebellion, escape, cultural survival, and community-building.

Cross-Regional Continuities & Change

  • Continuity: Native peoples continued adapting to their environments and defending their communities even after European arrival.
  • Change: European contact brought disease, conquest, Christianity, new animals, forced labor systems, and major demographic collapse.
  • Similarities: Spain, Portugal, and later England all pursued wealth, land, trade, and power in the Americas.
  • Differences: Spanish colonization focused heavily on conquest, Catholic missions, and extracting wealth, while later English colonization focused more on permanent settlements and agriculture.

📝 Multiple Choice Practice

Click an answer to check it. Each question includes an explanation.

1. Which of the following best explains why Native American societies in North America developed differently before European contact?

(A) All Native societies used the same farming methods and political systems.
(B) Different environments led Native peoples to develop different economies and settlement patterns.
(C) European trade goods shaped Native societies before 1491.
(D) Most Native groups lived in large empires controlled by one ruler.
Answer: B. Native societies adapted to their environments. The Southwest used irrigation farming, the Pacific Northwest relied heavily on fishing, and the Great Plains used more mobile hunting and gathering patterns.

2. The Columbian Exchange most directly caused which of the following?

(A) The end of European colonization in the Americas.
(B) The immediate creation of democratic governments in Native societies.
(C) The transfer of crops, animals, diseases, and people between hemispheres.
(D) The complete isolation of Europe from the Atlantic world.
Answer: C. The Columbian Exchange moved crops like maize and potatoes, animals like horses and cattle, and diseases like smallpox between the Eastern and Western Hemispheres.

3. Which of the following was a major reason for the rapid decline of Native populations after 1492?

(A) Native peoples stopped growing maize.
(B) European diseases spread among Native peoples who lacked immunity.
(C) Spanish colonists ended all forced labor systems.
(D) The Treaty of Tordesillas protected Native lands.
Answer: B. Diseases such as smallpox, measles, and influenza killed millions of Native people because they had no previous exposure and little immunity.

4. Which statement best describes the encomienda system?

(A) A Native American confederacy formed to resist English colonists.
(B) A Spanish labor system that required Native peoples to provide labor and tribute.
(C) A Portuguese agreement to stop colonizing Brazil.
(D) An English farming system based on independent family farms.
Answer: B. Under the encomienda system, Spanish colonists received the right to demand labor and tribute from Native peoples while claiming they would protect and Christianize them.

5. Which of the following best explains why Europeans increasingly used enslaved African labor in the Americas?

(A) European monarchs banned plantation agriculture.
(B) Native population decline and plantation labor demands increased the need for forced labor.
(C) African kingdoms controlled all land in North America.
(D) The Columbian Exchange ended sugar production.
Answer: B. As Native populations declined from disease and exploitation, Europeans turned increasingly to enslaved Africans, especially for sugar plantations in the Caribbean and Brazil.

✍️ Short-Answer Practice (SAQ)

SAQs ask you to respond in complete sentences — no thesis needed, but be specific and use evidence. Aim for 2–4 sentences per part.

SAQ 1 — Native Societies (No Stimulus)

  1. Identify ONE way the environment shaped Native American societies before European contact.
  2. Identify ONE difference between Native societies in the Southwest and Native societies in the Pacific Northwest.
  3. Explain ONE way maize agriculture affected Native societies before European contact.
Click to see a sample response

(a) The environment shaped Native societies because groups adapted their food sources and settlement patterns to local conditions. For example, Pacific Northwest peoples relied heavily on salmon and other marine resources.

(b) Southwestern Native societies such as the Pueblo used irrigation to farm maize in dry regions, while Pacific Northwest societies relied more on fishing and permanent coastal villages.

(c) Maize agriculture supported population growth and more permanent settlements. In places like the Mississippi River Valley, maize helped support larger societies such as Cahokia.

SAQ 2 — Stimulus-Based (Quote)

"The Spaniards have a perfect right to rule these barbarians of the New World... who in prudence, skill, virtues, and humanity are as inferior to the Spaniards as children to adults."
— Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda, c. 1550

  1. Identify ONE argument Sepúlveda used to justify Spanish colonization.
  2. Explain ONE way Spanish colonization affected Native peoples in the Americas.
  3. Explain ONE way another Spanish figure challenged Sepúlveda's view.
Click to see a sample response

(a) Sepúlveda argued that Spanish colonization was justified because he believed Native peoples were inferior to Europeans and needed Spanish rule.

(b) Spanish colonization forced many Native peoples into labor systems such as the encomienda, where they had to provide labor and tribute to Spanish colonists.

(c) Bartolomé de Las Casas challenged Sepúlveda by arguing that Native peoples were human beings who deserved humane treatment and should not be abused by Spanish colonists.

⭐ Key Takeaways

  • Native societies were diverse. Before European arrival, North America contained many different societies shaped by geography, climate, food sources, and trade.
  • Maize mattered. The spread of maize agriculture supported larger populations, settled villages, and complex societies in some regions.
  • European exploration had multiple causes. Europeans explored because of religion, wealth, competition, and new navigation technology.
  • The Columbian Exchange transformed the world. Crops, animals, diseases, people, and ideas moved between hemispheres after 1492.
  • Spanish colonization used forced labor and hierarchy. The encomienda system, missions, and casta system helped Spain control people and extract wealth.
  • African slavery expanded in the Atlantic world. Native population decline and plantation labor demand pushed Europeans toward the forced labor of enslaved Africans.
