Unit 8: Period 8

1945 – 1980  |  Exam Weight: 10–17%

🇺🇸 Unit Overview

APUSH Unit 8 covers the United States after World War II through the late 1970s. During this period, the U.S. became a global superpower, fought the Cold War, expanded the federal government at home, experienced major civil rights victories, and faced deep conflicts over Vietnam, race, gender, youth culture, and trust in government.

Big question: How did the Cold War, civil rights movement, Vietnam War, social movements, and economic problems reshape American society and government between 1945 and 1980?

Berlin Wall — connects to Cold War division between the U.S.-backed West and Soviet-controlled East.

Topic 8.1 — Contextualizing Period 8

🌎 Cold War America

After World War II, the United States became one of two global superpowers. The U.S. competed with the Soviet Union politically, economically, militarily, and ideologically. At home, Americans experienced prosperity, suburban growth, civil rights activism, and growing distrust of government.

Major Themes

  • Cold War: The U.S. tried to contain communism around the world.
  • Consumer prosperity: Many Americans moved to suburbs and joined the middle class.
  • Civil rights: African Americans and other groups challenged discrimination and inequality.
  • Vietnam War: The war divided Americans and weakened trust in government.
  • Social movements: Women, Latinos, Native Americans, gay rights activists, environmentalists, and youth movements demanded change.
  • 1970s crisis: Stagflation, oil shocks, Watergate, and foreign policy problems hurt confidence in government.

Topic 8.2 — The Cold War Begins

🧊 Containment and Superpower Rivalry

The Cold War was a long conflict between the United States and the Soviet Union. It was called “cold” because the two superpowers did not directly fight each other, but they competed through alliances, nuclear weapons, propaganda, and proxy wars.

Causes of the Cold War

  • Ideological conflict: The U.S. supported capitalism and democracy, while the Soviet Union supported communism and one-party rule.
  • Postwar Europe: The Soviet Union controlled Eastern Europe, creating fears of communist expansion.
  • Nuclear arms race: Both sides built powerful nuclear weapons.
  • Distrust: Wartime cooperation ended quickly after World War II.

Containment Policies

  • Containment: U.S. policy of stopping the spread of communism.
  • Truman Doctrine: Promised aid to countries resisting communism, first Greece and Turkey.
  • Marshall Plan: Gave economic aid to rebuild Western Europe and prevent communist influence.
  • NATO: Military alliance between the U.S., Canada, and Western Europe.
  • Berlin Airlift: U.S. supplied West Berlin by air after the Soviet blockade.
Berlin Airlift — connects to containment and early Cold War conflict in Europe.

Topic 8.3 — The Cold War at Home

🕵️ Red Scare and Anticommunism

The Cold War created fear of communist influence inside the United States. Americans worried about spies, nuclear war, and loyalty to the government.

Domestic Anticommunism

  • HUAC: House Un-American Activities Committee investigated suspected communists, especially in Hollywood and government.
  • Hollywood Ten: Film industry figures who refused to answer HUAC questions and were blacklisted.
  • McCarthyism: Senator Joseph McCarthy made dramatic accusations of communist infiltration, often without strong evidence.
  • Rosenbergs: Julius and Ethel Rosenberg were executed for passing atomic secrets to the Soviet Union.

Impact on Society

  • Fear of communism encouraged conformity and suspicion.
  • Some people lost jobs or reputations because of accusations.
  • Nuclear drills and fallout shelters reflected fear of nuclear war.

Topic 8.4 — Postwar Prosperity and the 1950s

🏡 Suburbs, Consumerism, and Conformity

After World War II, many Americans experienced economic prosperity. Federal programs, defense spending, suburban housing, and consumer culture helped create a large middle class.

Postwar Economic Growth

  • GI Bill: Helped veterans pay for college, buy homes, and start businesses.
  • Baby boom: Birth rates increased after World War II.
  • Suburbs: Communities like Levittown offered affordable homes for many white middle-class families.
  • Consumer culture: Americans bought cars, televisions, appliances, and suburban homes.
  • Interstate Highway Act: Built highways that encouraged suburbs, car culture, and national transportation.

Limits of Prosperity

  • Many Black Americans, Latinos, Native Americans, and poor whites were excluded from full prosperity.
  • Redlining and housing discrimination limited where minorities could live.
  • Women were often pressured into domestic roles despite wartime work experience.
Levittown — connects to suburbanization, consumer culture, and the postwar middle class.

Topic 8.5 — The Civil Rights Movement

✊ Fighting Segregation and Discrimination

The civil rights movement challenged Jim Crow segregation, voter suppression, and racial inequality. Activists used lawsuits, boycotts, sit-ins, marches, and civil disobedience to pressure the government and public opinion.

Legal and Political Victories

  • Brown v. Board of Education: Declared school segregation unconstitutional.
  • Civil Rights Act of 1964: Banned segregation in public accommodations and employment discrimination.
  • Voting Rights Act of 1965: Banned literacy tests and gave the federal government power to protect voting rights.
  • 24th Amendment: Banned poll taxes in federal elections.

Activism

  • Montgomery Bus Boycott: Protested bus segregation and helped make Martin Luther King Jr. a national leader.
  • Sit-ins: Students protested segregated lunch counters.
  • Freedom Rides: Activists challenged segregation in interstate bus travel.
  • March on Washington: Demonstration for jobs and freedom where MLK gave his “I Have a Dream” speech.
  • Selma marches: Helped push Congress to pass the Voting Rights Act.

Different Approaches

  • Martin Luther King Jr.: Supported nonviolent civil disobedience and integration.
  • Malcolm X: Criticized gradualism and emphasized Black pride and self-defense.
  • Black Power: Called for racial pride, political power, and community control.
March on Washington — connects to civil rights activism, jobs, freedom, and MLK's leadership.

Topic 8.6 — The Great Society and Liberal Reform

🏛️ Expanding the Federal Government

President Lyndon B. Johnson's Great Society expanded the federal government's role in fighting poverty, improving health care, supporting education, and protecting civil rights.

Great Society Programs

  • War on Poverty: Federal effort to reduce poverty through jobs, education, and community programs.
  • Medicare: Health insurance for elderly Americans.
  • Medicaid: Health insurance for low-income Americans.
  • Elementary and Secondary Education Act: Increased federal aid to schools.
  • Immigration Act of 1965: Ended the national origins quota system and increased immigration from Asia, Latin America, and Africa.

Limits and Criticism

  • The Vietnam War drained money and attention away from domestic reform.
  • Conservatives criticized Great Society programs as too expensive and too large.
  • Some activists argued reforms did not go far enough to fix structural inequality.

Topic 8.7 — The Vietnam War

🌏 Containment in Southeast Asia

The Vietnam War was part of the Cold War policy of containment. U.S. leaders believed they had to stop communist expansion in Southeast Asia, but the war became deeply unpopular and divided the country.

Causes and Escalation

  • Domino theory: Belief that if one country became communist, nearby countries would follow.
  • Geneva Accords: Temporarily divided Vietnam into communist North Vietnam and noncommunist South Vietnam.
  • Gulf of Tonkin Resolution: Gave President Johnson broad authority to expand the war.
  • Escalation: The U.S. sent hundreds of thousands of troops to Vietnam.

Opposition and Turning Points

  • Tet Offensive: Major communist attack in 1968 that shocked Americans and weakened support for the war.
  • Credibility gap: Difference between government claims and what Americans saw on television.
  • Antiwar movement: Students, activists, veterans, and others protested the war.
  • Kent State: National Guard killed four students during an antiwar protest in 1970.

End of the War

  • Vietnamization: Nixon's policy of turning more fighting over to South Vietnamese forces.
  • Paris Peace Accords: U.S. withdrew combat troops in 1973.
  • Fall of Saigon: North Vietnam captured South Vietnam in 1975.
  • War Powers Act: Tried to limit presidential power to send troops without Congress.
Vietnam War protest — connects to antiwar activism and distrust of government.

Topic 8.8 — Social Movements of the 1960s and 1970s

🌈 New Movements for Rights and Identity

The civil rights movement inspired many other groups to demand equality, recognition, and political power. The 1960s and 1970s saw major activism around gender, ethnicity, sexuality, environment, and youth culture.

Women's Movement

  • Betty Friedan: Wrote The Feminine Mystique, criticizing the limited domestic role expected of women.
  • NOW: National Organization for Women fought workplace discrimination and legal inequality.
  • Title IX: Banned sex discrimination in federally funded education programs.
  • Roe v. Wade: Supreme Court protected abortion rights in 1973.

Latino, Native American, and Gay Rights Movements

  • César Chávez: Organized farmworkers through the United Farm Workers.
  • Chicano movement: Fought for Mexican American rights, education, labor protections, and cultural pride.
  • American Indian Movement: Demanded Native sovereignty and protested broken treaties.
  • Stonewall uprising: Helped energize the modern gay rights movement.

Environmental Movement

  • Rachel Carson: Silent Spring warned about pesticides and environmental damage.
  • Earth Day: First celebrated in 1970.
  • EPA: Environmental Protection Agency created to regulate pollution.
  • Clean Air Act and Clean Water Act: Expanded environmental regulation.
César Chávez — connects to Latino activism, farmworker organizing, and the United Farm Workers.

Topic 8.9 — The 1970s: Crisis and Conservative Reaction

📉 Stagflation, Watergate, and Distrust

The 1970s brought economic problems, political scandal, and foreign policy setbacks. Many Americans lost trust in government and began questioning liberal policies from earlier decades.

Economic Problems

  • Stagflation: Combination of inflation and unemployment.
  • Oil crisis: OPEC oil embargo caused gas shortages and higher prices.
  • Deindustrialization: Some manufacturing jobs declined, especially in older industrial cities.

Watergate

  • Break-in: Nixon's campaign connected to a break-in at Democratic headquarters.
  • Cover-up: Nixon tried to hide White House involvement.
  • Resignation: Nixon resigned in 1974 before he could be impeached.
  • Impact: Watergate increased distrust of the presidency and federal government.

Foreign Policy

  • Détente: Nixon eased tensions with the Soviet Union and opened relations with China.
  • SALT I: Arms control agreement with the Soviet Union.
  • Camp David Accords: Carter helped negotiate peace between Egypt and Israel.
  • Iranian hostage crisis: Americans were held hostage in Iran, weakening Carter's presidency.
Nixon leaving the White House — connects to Watergate and declining trust in government.

Cross-Regional Continuities & Change

  • Continuity: The U.S. continued to debate federal power, civil rights, foreign intervention, and economic inequality.

  • Differences: Cold War containment had broad support in the 1950s, but Vietnam created major public opposition by the late 1960s.

📝 Multiple Choice Practice

Click an answer to check it. Each question includes an explanation.

1. Which policy best describes the main U.S. strategy during the Cold War?

(A) Isolationism from all world affairs.
(B) Containment of communism.
(C) Immediate alliance with the Soviet Union.
(D) Complete disarmament after World War II.
Answer: B. Containment was the main U.S. Cold War policy, aiming to stop communism from spreading to new regions.

2. Which Supreme Court case declared school segregation unconstitutional?

(A) Plessy v. Ferguson.
(B) Brown v. Board of Education.
(C) Roe v. Wade.
(D) Worcester v. Georgia.
Answer: B. Brown v. Board of Education overturned school segregation by ruling that separate schools were inherently unequal.

3. Which of the following was a major effect of the Vietnam War on American society?

(A) It ended all protest movements.
(B) It increased distrust of government and fueled antiwar activism.
(C) It eliminated the Cold War.
(D) It caused the United States to join NATO for the first time.
Answer: B. The Vietnam War created a credibility gap, inspired protest, and weakened public trust in government.

4. Which program was part of Lyndon B. Johnson's Great Society?

(A) Marshall Plan.
(B) Dawes Act.
(C) Medicare.
(D) Homestead Act.
Answer: C. Medicare was created under the Great Society to provide health insurance for elderly Americans.

5. Watergate is most closely associated with which historical development?

(A) Expansion of trust in the presidency.
(B) Nixon's resignation and increased distrust of government.
(C) The beginning of the Korean War.
(D) The creation of the GI Bill.
Answer: B. The Watergate scandal led to Nixon's resignation and deepened public distrust of government.

⭐ Key Takeaways

  • The Cold War shaped foreign and domestic policy. Containment led to alliances, aid programs, proxy wars, nuclear buildup, and anticommunism at home.
  • Postwar prosperity expanded the middle class. Suburbs, consumer goods, highways, and the GI Bill changed American life.
  • Prosperity was unequal. Discrimination and poverty remained serious problems for many groups.
  • The civil rights movement transformed federal law. Brown, the Civil Rights Act, and the Voting Rights Act attacked segregation and voter suppression.
  • The Great Society expanded government responsibility. Medicare, Medicaid, education aid, and anti-poverty programs increased federal involvement in society.
  • Vietnam divided the country. The war created protests, a credibility gap, and new limits on presidential war powers.
  • The 1970s weakened trust in government. Stagflation, oil crises, Watergate, and foreign policy problems contributed to conservative backlash.
