Unit 2: Period 2

1607 – 1754  |  Exam Weight: 6–8%

🇺🇸 Unit Overview

APUSH Unit 2 covers the growth of European colonies in North America from the founding of Jamestown to the beginning of the French and Indian War. During this period, Spanish, French, Dutch, and English colonies developed different goals, economies, labor systems, and relationships with Native Americans. The English colonies grew especially quickly and became divided into distinct regional societies in New England, the Middle Colonies, the Chesapeake, and the Southern Colonies.

Big question: How did European colonies in North America develop differently, and how did those differences shape labor systems, Native relations, religion, economics, and colonial society between 1607 and 1754?

Topic 2.1 — Contextualizing Period 2

🌎 Europe Competes for North America

After 1607, European colonization in North America became more permanent and competitive. Different European powers used colonies to gain wealth, spread religion, increase trade, and expand imperial power.

Big Trends

  • European rivalry: Spain, France, the Netherlands, and England competed for land, trade, and influence.
  • Mercantilism: European powers believed colonies existed to enrich the mother country through raw materials, trade, and markets.
  • Native diplomacy and resistance: Native peoples traded with, allied with, and fought against Europeans to protect their interests.
  • Labor systems: Colonies used different types of labor, including family labor, indentured servitude, Native labor, and enslaved African labor.

Why This Matters

  • Colonial regions developed differently because of climate, geography, religion, labor needs, and economic goals.
  • Differences between colonial regions later shaped politics, identity, and sectional conflict.
  • This period sets up major conflicts over empire, slavery, and self-government.

Topic 2.2 — European Colonization

👑 Spanish, French, Dutch, and English Colonies

European colonies were not all the same. Each empire had different priorities, which shaped its economy, settlement patterns, and relationships with Native Americans.

Spanish Colonies

  • Main goals: Extract wealth, spread Catholicism, and maintain imperial control.
  • Location: Florida, New Mexico, Texas, California, Mexico, the Caribbean, and South America.
  • Mission system: Catholic missions were used to convert Native peoples and organize labor.
  • Native resistance: The Pueblo Revolt of 1680 showed that Native peoples resisted Spanish religious and labor control.

French Colonies

  • Main goals: Fur trade, Catholic missionary work, and alliances with Native peoples.
  • Location: Canada, Great Lakes, Mississippi River Valley, and Louisiana.
  • Settlement pattern: Smaller population than English colonies because France focused more on trade than mass settlement.
  • Native relations: French traders often depended on Native alliances and intermarriage for the fur trade.

Dutch Colonies

  • Main goals: Trade and profit.
  • New Netherland: Centered on New Amsterdam, later renamed New York after English takeover in 1664.
  • Diversity: Dutch colonies were religiously and ethnically diverse because of trade connections.

English Colonies

  • Main goals: Settlement, agriculture, trade, religious communities, and economic opportunity.
  • Population growth: English colonies attracted more settlers and grew faster than French and Spanish colonies in North America.
  • Regional differences: New England, Middle, Chesapeake, and Southern colonies developed distinct economies and cultures.

Topic 2.3 — The Regions of British Colonies

🏘️ Regional Development in English North America

The English colonies developed into different regions because of climate, geography, religion, labor systems, and economic goals.

New England Colonies

  • Colonies: Massachusetts, Connecticut, Rhode Island, New Hampshire.
  • Religion: Puritanism shaped government, education, and daily life in Massachusetts.
  • Economy: Fishing, shipbuilding, small farms, lumber, and trade.
  • Society: Towns, families, churches, schools, and relatively longer life expectancy.
  • Dissenters: Roger Williams and Anne Hutchinson challenged Puritan authority and helped expand religious diversity.

Middle Colonies

  • Colonies: New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Delaware.
  • Economy: Grain farming, trade, ports, and small manufacturing.
  • Diversity: Many ethnic and religious groups, including Dutch, Germans, Quakers, Jews, and others.
  • Pennsylvania: Founded by William Penn as a Quaker colony with religious tolerance and better relations with Native peoples at first.

Chesapeake Colonies

  • Colonies: Virginia and Maryland.
  • Economy: Tobacco plantations became the center of colonial life.
  • Labor: First relied heavily on indentured servants, then shifted toward enslaved African labor.
  • Society: More spread-out plantations, wealthy planters, fewer towns, and high death rates early on.

Southern Colonies

  • Colonies: North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia.
  • Economy: Rice, indigo, and plantation agriculture.
  • Labor: Enslaved African labor became central, especially in South Carolina.
  • Caribbean influence: Plantation slavery in the South was influenced by Barbados and other Caribbean colonies.

Topic 2.4 — Transatlantic Trade

🚢 The Atlantic Economy

By the 1600s and 1700s, the Atlantic Ocean connected Europe, Africa, and the Americas through trade, migration, and forced labor. Colonies produced raw materials while European nations tried to control colonial commerce.

Mercantilism

  • Definition: Economic system where colonies exist to benefit the mother country.
  • Goal: Export more than import, gain wealth, and control trade.
  • Colonial role: Colonies provided raw materials and bought manufactured goods from the mother country.

Navigation Acts

  • Purpose: England passed these laws to control colonial trade and make sure profits benefited England.
  • Rules: Certain goods had to be shipped on English ships and sold through English ports.
  • Effect: These acts increased English control but also encouraged smuggling and colonial resentment.

Triangular Trade

  • Europe to Africa: Manufactured goods were traded for enslaved Africans.
  • Africa to Americas: Enslaved Africans were forced across the Atlantic through the Middle Passage.
  • Americas to Europe: Raw materials like sugar, tobacco, rice, indigo, and rum entered Atlantic markets.

Topic 2.5 — Interactions Between American Indians and Europeans

🪶 Cooperation, Conflict, and Resistance

Native Americans were not passive during colonization. They made alliances, traded, negotiated, adapted, and resisted European expansion.

Native-European Trade

  • Fur trade: French and Dutch traders relied heavily on Native hunters and alliances.
  • European goods: Native peoples gained access to guns, metal tools, textiles, and alcohol.
  • Power shifts: Trade goods changed Native warfare, diplomacy, and relationships between tribes.

Conflict Over Land

  • English settlement: English colonies usually brought more settlers, which created more direct land conflict with Native peoples.
  • Pequot War: Conflict in New England that showed English willingness to use extreme violence to expand control.
  • King Philip's War: A major Native resistance war led by Metacom that devastated Native power in southern New England.

Spanish-Native Conflict

  • Pueblo Revolt: In 1680, Pueblo peoples rebelled against Spanish religious suppression and forced labor.
  • Result: Spanish colonists were forced out of New Mexico temporarily.
  • Significance: It was one of the most successful Native uprisings against European colonization.

Topic 2.6 — Slavery in the British Colonies

⛓️ Slavery and Colonial Labor

During the 1600s, English colonies shifted from relying mainly on indentured servants to relying more heavily on enslaved African labor, especially in plantation colonies.

Indentured Servitude

  • Definition: Workers signed contracts to work for several years in exchange for passage to America.
  • Used heavily in: Chesapeake tobacco colonies like Virginia and Maryland.
  • Problems: Former servants often became poor landless men, creating social tension.

Bacon's Rebellion

  • Cause: Frontier settlers were angry about Native policy, land access, taxes, and elite control.
  • Leader: Nathaniel Bacon led poor farmers, former indentured servants, and some enslaved Africans against Virginia's government.
  • Significance: Virginia elites became more fearful of poor white rebellion and increasingly turned to enslaved African labor.

Growth of Racial Slavery

  • Slave codes: Colonies passed laws that made slavery permanent, hereditary, and race-based.
  • Plantation colonies: Tobacco, rice, and indigo plantations depended heavily on enslaved labor.
  • Resistance: Enslaved people resisted through work slowdowns, escape, cultural survival, and rebellion.
  • Stono Rebellion: 1739 uprising in South Carolina that led to harsher slave laws.

Topic 2.7 — Colonial Society and Culture

📜 Religion, Self-Government, and Identity

By the mid-1700s, British colonists were developing their own regional cultures, political traditions, religious ideas, and economic interests.

Self-Government

  • House of Burgesses: Created in Virginia in 1619; early example of representative government.
  • Mayflower Compact: Agreement by Plymouth settlers to create a government based on consent.
  • Town meetings: New England communities practiced local self-government.
  • Salutary neglect: England often loosely enforced trade laws, allowing colonies to develop more independence.

Religion

  • Puritans: Wanted to create a godly society in New England.
  • Religious toleration: Rhode Island and Pennsylvania became known for greater religious freedom.
  • First Great Awakening: Religious revival movement that emphasized emotional preaching, personal conversion, and challenged traditional church authority.

Enlightenment Ideas

  • Reason and science: Enlightenment thinkers emphasized logic, natural laws, and human progress.
  • John Locke: Ideas about natural rights and government by consent influenced later American political thought.
  • Benjamin Franklin: Represented Enlightenment values through science, invention, writing, and civic improvement.

  • Differences: French colonies focused more on trade and alliances, while English colonies focused more on settlement and agriculture.

📝 Multiple Choice Practice

Click an answer to check it. Each question includes an explanation.

1. Which of the following best explains why the French generally had better relations with many Native groups than the English did?

(A) The French brought more settlers and took more Native land than the English.
(B) The French depended more on fur trade alliances and had fewer permanent settlers.
(C) The French refused to trade with Native peoples.
(D) The French colonies were located only in the Caribbean.
Answer: B. French colonies had smaller populations and relied heavily on Native trade partners for the fur trade, so alliances were more important to them than mass settlement.

2. Which colonial region was most closely associated with Puritan religious communities, town meetings, fishing, and shipbuilding?

(A) New England.
(B) Chesapeake.
(C) Spanish Florida.
(D) French Louisiana.
Answer: A. New England colonies like Massachusetts were shaped by Puritanism, town-based settlement, fishing, shipbuilding, trade, and small farms.

3. Bacon's Rebellion most directly contributed to which of the following developments?

(A) The end of tobacco farming in Virginia.
(B) The permanent alliance between English settlers and Native peoples.
(C) Greater reliance on enslaved African labor instead of indentured servitude.
(D) The creation of the First Great Awakening.
Answer: C. After Bacon's Rebellion, Virginia elites worried about poor white indentured servants and former servants rebelling, so they increasingly relied on enslaved African labor.

4. Which statement best describes mercantilism?

(A) Colonies should be politically independent from the mother country.
(B) Colonies exist to provide raw materials and wealth for the mother country.
(C) Native peoples should control all colonial trade.
(D) Governments should ban all overseas trade.
Answer: B. Mercantilism was an economic system where European nations used colonies to gain raw materials, markets, and wealth.

5. Which event was a successful Native uprising against Spanish rule in New Mexico?

(A) Bacon's Rebellion.
(B) Stono Rebellion.
(C) Pueblo Revolt.
(D) Glorious Revolution.
Answer: C. The Pueblo Revolt of 1680 forced Spanish colonists out of New Mexico temporarily and was one of the most successful Native revolts against European colonization.

⭐ Key Takeaways

  • European empires colonized differently. Spain emphasized conquest and missions, France emphasized fur trade, the Dutch emphasized commerce, and England emphasized settlement and agriculture.
  • English colonies developed regional differences. New England, Middle, Chesapeake, and Southern colonies had different economies, religions, labor systems, and settlement patterns.
  • Native peoples resisted and adapted. Native Americans traded, allied, negotiated, and fought to protect their land and power.
  • Slavery expanded. Labor systems shifted from indentured servitude toward race-based African slavery, especially in plantation colonies.
  • Colonial self-government grew. Assemblies, town meetings, and salutary neglect helped colonists develop political habits that later mattered in the American Revolution.
  • Religion shaped colonial identity. Puritanism, religious toleration, and the First Great Awakening influenced colonial society and culture.
