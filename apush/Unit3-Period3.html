Unit 3: Period 3

1754 – 1800  |  Exam Weight: 10–17%

🇺🇸 Unit Overview

APUSH Unit 3 covers the road to the American Revolution, the Revolution itself, the creation of the United States government, and the early republic. This period begins with the French and Indian War, which changed the relationship between Britain and its colonies. It then follows colonial resistance, independence, the Articles of Confederation, the Constitution, Washington's presidency, the rise of political parties, and growing debates over the meaning of the Revolution.

Big question: How did imperial conflict, Enlightenment ideas, colonial resistance, and debates over government lead to the American Revolution and the creation of a new republic between 1754 and 1800?

Topic 3.1 — Contextualizing Period 3

🌎 From British Colonies to New Nation

Period 3 is about how British colonists became Americans. The period starts with Britain winning a huge empire after the French and Indian War, but that victory created debt, conflict, and new imperial policies that angered colonists.

Major Themes

  • Imperial conflict: Britain and France fought for control of North America during the French and Indian War.
  • Taxation and representation: Colonists resisted British taxes because they lacked direct representation in Parliament.
  • Enlightenment ideas: Natural rights, consent of the governed, and republicanism shaped revolutionary thinking.
  • Debates over power: Americans argued over how strong the national government should be.

Why This Matters

  • The American Revolution was both a war for independence and a debate over political ideals.
  • The Constitution created a stronger federal government than the Articles of Confederation.
  • Early political conflicts over banks, taxes, foreign policy, and states' rights shaped U.S. politics for decades.

Topic 3.2 — The Seven Years' War / French and Indian War

⚔️ War Changes the British Empire

The French and Indian War was the North American part of the larger Seven Years' War. Britain and France fought over land, trade, and control of the Ohio River Valley.

Causes

  • Ohio River Valley: Both Britain and France wanted control of this valuable region.
  • Imperial rivalry: Britain and France competed globally for empire and trade.
  • Native alliances: Many Native groups allied with France because French settlement was smaller and focused more on trade.

Results

  • Treaty of Paris 1763: Britain gained Canada and land east of the Mississippi River from France.
  • British debt: The war was expensive, so Britain began taxing colonists more directly.
  • Proclamation of 1763: Britain tried to stop colonists from settling west of the Appalachian Mountains to avoid conflict with Native peoples.
  • Colonial resentment: Colonists were angry because they believed they had helped win western lands but were blocked from settling them.

Topic 3.3 — Taxation Without Representation

📜 British Policies and Colonial Resistance

After 1763, Britain tried to tighten control over the colonies and raise revenue. Colonists responded with protests, boycotts, petitions, and eventually armed resistance.

British Policies

  • Sugar Act: Taxed sugar and molasses to raise revenue.
  • Stamp Act: Taxed paper goods like newspapers, legal documents, and playing cards.
  • Townshend Acts: Taxed imported goods such as glass, paper, paint, lead, and tea.
  • Tea Act: Gave the British East India Company an advantage in the tea trade.
  • Coercive/Intolerable Acts: Punished Massachusetts after the Boston Tea Party.

Colonial Arguments

  • No taxation without representation: Colonists argued Parliament could not tax them because they had no elected representatives there.
  • Rights of Englishmen: Colonists claimed they deserved the same legal rights as British citizens.
  • Virtual representation rejected: Britain argued Parliament represented all British subjects, but colonists rejected this idea.

Resistance

  • Sons of Liberty: Organized protests against British policies.
  • Daughters of Liberty: Supported boycotts by making homespun cloth and avoiding British goods.
  • Committees of Correspondence: Spread political information between colonies.
  • Continental Congress: Colonial leaders met to coordinate resistance.

Topic 3.4 — Philosophical Foundations of the American Revolution

💡 Enlightenment and Republican Ideas

The American Revolution was influenced by Enlightenment ideas about natural rights, government by consent, and the right to resist tyranny.

Important Ideas

  • Natural rights: John Locke argued that people have rights to life, liberty, and property.
  • Consent of the governed: Governments get power from the people.
  • Social contract: People agree to form governments to protect their rights.
  • Right of revolution: If a government violates rights, people can change or overthrow it.
  • Republicanism: A government should be based on elected representatives and civic virtue.

Key Documents

  • Common Sense: Thomas Paine argued that monarchy was corrupt and independence made sense.
  • Declaration of Independence: Written mostly by Thomas Jefferson, it argued that all men are created equal and listed grievances against King George III.
  • State constitutions: Many states wrote constitutions that expanded republican government but still limited political rights for women, enslaved people, and many poor men.

Topic 3.5 — The American Revolution

🗽 War for Independence

The American Revolution was fought between Patriot colonists and Britain, but not all colonists supported independence. The war also involved Loyalists, Native Americans, enslaved people, women, and foreign allies.

Patriots, Loyalists, and Others

  • Patriots: Supported independence from Britain.
  • Loyalists: Remained loyal to Britain, often because of economic ties, fear of disorder, or belief in monarchy.
  • Native Americans: Many sided with Britain because they hoped Britain would limit American westward expansion.
  • Enslaved African Americans: Some fought for Britain because Britain offered freedom to enslaved people who escaped Patriot masters.
  • Women: Supported the war by boycotting goods, managing farms and businesses, making supplies, and sometimes serving near armies.

Major Turning Points

  • Lexington and Concord: First battles of the Revolutionary War.
  • Bunker Hill: Showed colonists could fight British regulars, even though Britain won the battle.
  • Saratoga: Major American victory that convinced France to ally with the United States.
  • Yorktown: Final major battle where British General Cornwallis surrendered in 1781.

Why America Won

  • French alliance: France provided troops, ships, money, and supplies.
  • Home-field advantage: Patriots knew the land and had local support.
  • British difficulties: Britain fought far from home and struggled to control a large territory.
  • Washington's leadership: George Washington kept the Continental Army together during difficult years.

Topic 3.6 — The Influence of Revolutionary Ideals

⚖️ Freedom, Equality, and Contradictions

The Revolution promoted ideas of liberty and equality, but those ideals were applied unevenly. Many groups used revolutionary language to challenge inequality, even when they did not immediately gain full rights.

Women

  • Republican motherhood: Women were expected to raise educated, virtuous sons for the republic.
  • Abigail Adams: Asked John Adams to “remember the ladies,” showing that some women challenged male political power.
  • Limits: Women generally did not gain voting rights or equal legal status.

African Americans

  • Revolutionary contradiction: A nation built on liberty still allowed slavery.
  • Northern gradual emancipation: Some northern states began passing laws to slowly end slavery.
  • Southern slavery: Slavery remained deeply rooted in the South.

Native Americans

  • Western expansion: After independence, American settlers pushed westward onto Native lands.
  • Limited protection: Native peoples were generally excluded from the new republic's political system.
  • Ongoing resistance: Native groups continued resisting U.S. expansion in the Ohio Valley and beyond.

Topic 3.7 — The Articles of Confederation

📄 America's First Government

The Articles of Confederation created the first national government of the United States. Americans feared tyranny, so they made the national government weak and gave most power to the states.

Strengths

  • Won the Revolution: The Articles government helped guide the country through the end of the war.
  • Land Ordinance of 1785: Created a system for surveying and selling western lands.
  • Northwest Ordinance of 1787: Created a process for territories to become states and banned slavery in the Northwest Territory.

Weaknesses

  • No power to tax: The national government could request money but not force states to pay.
  • No executive branch: There was no president to enforce laws.
  • No national court system: There was no strong federal judiciary.
  • Hard to amend: Changes required unanimous state approval.
  • Weak military: The national government struggled to respond to rebellions or threats.

Shays' Rebellion

  • Cause: Farmers in Massachusetts protested debt, taxes, and foreclosures.
  • Significance: The rebellion convinced many elites that the Articles were too weak and needed replacement.

Topic 3.8 — The Constitutional Convention and Debates Over Ratification

🏛️ Creating the Constitution

In 1787, delegates met in Philadelphia to revise the Articles of Confederation. Instead, they created a new Constitution with a stronger federal government.

Major Compromises

  • Great Compromise: Created a bicameral legislature with the House based on population and the Senate giving each state equal representation.
  • Three-Fifths Compromise: Counted three-fifths of enslaved people for representation and taxation, increasing Southern political power.
  • Electoral College: Created an indirect system for electing the president.

Principles of the Constitution

  • Federalism: Power is divided between national and state governments.
  • Separation of powers: Government is divided into legislative, executive, and judicial branches.
  • Checks and balances: Each branch can limit the power of the others.
  • Popular sovereignty: The government's authority comes from the people.

Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists

  • Federalists: Supported the Constitution and wanted a stronger national government. Key figures included Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay.
  • Anti-Federalists: Opposed or feared the Constitution because it gave too much power to the national government and lacked a bill of rights.
  • Bill of Rights: Added in 1791 to protect individual liberties and help secure ratification.

Topic 3.9 — The Early Republic

🦅 Washington, Hamilton, Jefferson, and Adams

After the Constitution was ratified, the United States had to prove that the new government could actually work. Washington's presidency set important precedents, while debates over Hamilton's financial plan and foreign policy created the first political parties.

Washington's Presidency

  • Cabinet: Washington created a group of advisers, including Hamilton as Secretary of the Treasury and Jefferson as Secretary of State.
  • Two-term precedent: Washington stepped down after two terms, setting an important example.
  • Neutrality Proclamation: Washington tried to keep the U.S. out of war between France and Britain.
  • Whiskey Rebellion: Washington used federal power to enforce a tax, showing the new government was stronger than under the Articles.

Hamilton's Financial Plan

  • Assumption of state debts: Federal government would take on state debts from the Revolution.
  • National bank: Hamilton wanted a Bank of the United States to stabilize the economy.
  • Protective tariffs: Hamilton wanted tariffs to support American manufacturing.
  • Loose construction: Hamilton argued the Constitution allowed implied powers through the necessary and proper clause.

Rise of Political Parties

  • Federalists: Led by Hamilton and Adams; supported strong federal government, national bank, manufacturing, and closer ties with Britain.
  • Democratic-Republicans: Led by Jefferson and Madison; supported states' rights, agriculture, strict construction, and sympathy for France.

Adams Presidency

  • XYZ Affair: French officials demanded bribes from American diplomats, increasing tensions with France.
  • Alien and Sedition Acts: Federalist laws made it harder for immigrants to become citizens and punished criticism of the government.
  • Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions: Jefferson and Madison argued that states could challenge unconstitutional federal laws.
  • Election of 1800: Jefferson defeated Adams, marking the first peaceful transfer of power between political parties.

📝 Multiple Choice Practice

Click an answer to check it. Each question includes an explanation.

1. Which of the following was a major effect of the French and Indian War on the relationship between Britain and its colonies?

(A) Britain ended all efforts to control colonial trade.
(B) Britain tried to tax and regulate the colonies more strictly to pay war debts.
(C) The colonies immediately gained independence from Britain.
(D) France gained control of all British colonies.
Answer: B. The war left Britain deeply in debt, so Parliament tried to raise revenue from the colonies through laws like the Sugar Act and Stamp Act, increasing colonial resistance.

2. The phrase “no taxation without representation” was mainly a protest against which British policy?

(A) Allowing colonists to elect members of Parliament.
(B) Ending all taxes on imported goods.
(C) Taxing colonists without their direct representation in Parliament.
(D) Giving Native Americans control over colonial assemblies.
Answer: C. Colonists argued that Parliament could not tax them because they had no elected representatives in Parliament.

3. Which Enlightenment idea most directly influenced the Declaration of Independence?

(A) Absolute monarchy protects liberty best.
(B) People have natural rights and may alter a government that violates them.
(C) Government power should come only from hereditary rulers.
(D) Colonies should never trade with other nations.
Answer: B. The Declaration used John Locke's ideas of natural rights, consent of the governed, and the right to revolution.

4. Which of the following best explains why the Constitution replaced the Articles of Confederation?

(A) The Articles gave the national government too much power over the states.
(B) The Articles created a weak national government that could not tax, regulate trade, or respond effectively to crises.
(C) The Articles banned all western settlement.
(D) The Articles created a powerful executive branch.
Answer: B. The Articles created a weak central government. Shays' Rebellion helped convince many leaders that a stronger federal government was needed.

5. Which issue most clearly divided Hamilton's Federalists from Jefferson's Democratic-Republicans?

(A) Whether the United States should return to British rule.
(B) Whether the federal government should be strong and support a national bank.
(C) Whether the Declaration of Independence should be repealed.
(D) Whether the Constitution should ban all political debate.
Answer: B. Hamilton's Federalists supported a strong national government, national bank, and commercial economy, while Jefferson's Democratic-Republicans favored states' rights, strict construction, and agriculture.

⭐ Key Takeaways

  • The French and Indian War changed everything. Britain's victory created debt and led to stricter colonial policies, which increased resistance.
  • Colonists protested taxation without representation. Resistance grew from petitions and boycotts to armed conflict and independence.
  • Enlightenment ideas shaped the Revolution. Natural rights, consent of the governed, and republicanism influenced American political thought.
  • The Revolution had limits. It expanded ideas of liberty but did not create equal rights for women, enslaved people, or Native Americans.
  • The Articles were too weak for many leaders. Shays' Rebellion and financial problems pushed Americans toward the Constitution.
  • The Constitution created a stronger federal government. Federalism, checks and balances, and the Bill of Rights became central features of the new system.
  • Political parties emerged quickly. Federalists and Democratic-Republicans disagreed over federal power, the economy, foreign policy, and constitutional interpretation.
