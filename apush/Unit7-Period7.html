Unit 7: Period 7

1890 – 1945  |  Exam Weight: 10–17%

🇺🇸 Unit Overview

APUSH Unit 7 covers the United States becoming a modern industrial, reform-minded, and global power. This period includes Progressivism, overseas imperialism, World War I, the cultural conflicts of the 1920s, the Great Depression, the New Deal, and World War II. The federal government became more involved in the economy, society, and global affairs than ever before.

Big question: How did reform, war, economic crisis, and global conflict transform the role of the federal government and the United States in the world between 1890 and 1945?

Topic 7.1 — Contextualizing Period 7

🌎 Modern America Emerges

From 1890 to 1945, the United States became more urban, industrial, reform-oriented, and internationally powerful. The government increasingly responded to social problems, economic crises, and global wars.

Major Themes

  • Progressive reform: Reformers tried to fix problems caused by industrialization, urbanization, corruption, and inequality.
  • Imperialism: The U.S. gained overseas territories and became more involved in world affairs.
  • War and diplomacy: World War I and World War II transformed America's global role.
  • Economic crisis: The Great Depression led to a major expansion of federal power through the New Deal.
  • Cultural conflict: The 1920s saw tensions over immigration, religion, race, gender, and modern culture.

Topic 7.2 — The Progressive Era

🧹 Reforming Industrial America

Progressives believed government and society could fix the problems created by industrialization and urbanization. They focused on political corruption, unsafe workplaces, poverty, monopolies, public health, and democracy.

Goals of Progressivism

  • Regulate big business: Progressives wanted to limit monopolies and protect consumers.
  • Protect workers: Reformers pushed for shorter hours, safer conditions, and limits on child labor.
  • Clean up government: Progressives fought political machines and supported direct democracy.
  • Improve cities: Settlement houses, sanitation reforms, and public health efforts helped urban communities.

Muckrakers

  • Upton Sinclair: Exposed the meatpacking industry in The Jungle.
  • Ida Tarbell: Criticized Standard Oil's unfair business practices.
  • Jacob Riis: Exposed urban poverty and tenement housing.
  • Lincoln Steffens: Exposed corruption in city governments.

Progressive Amendments

  • 16th Amendment: Federal income tax.
  • 17th Amendment: Direct election of senators.
  • 18th Amendment: Prohibition of alcohol.
  • 19th Amendment: Women's suffrage.

Topic 7.3 — Imperialism and the Spanish-American War

🌍 The U.S. Becomes a World Power

In the late 1800s, the United States expanded beyond the continent and became an overseas empire. Supporters believed imperialism would increase trade, military power, and national prestige.

Causes of Imperialism

  • Economic interests: Businesses wanted new markets and raw materials.
  • Military strategy: Alfred Thayer Mahan argued that a strong navy and overseas bases were needed for power.
  • Social Darwinism: Some Americans believed stronger nations had the right to dominate weaker ones.
  • Missionary motives: Some wanted to spread Christianity and American culture.

Spanish-American War

  • Causes: Cuban rebellion, yellow journalism, business interests, and the explosion of the USS Maine.
  • Result: The U.S. defeated Spain quickly and gained Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines.
  • Debate: Anti-imperialists argued empire violated American ideals of self-government.

U.S. Influence in Latin America and Asia

  • Open Door Policy: Called for equal trading rights in China.
  • Roosevelt Corollary: Claimed the U.S. could intervene in Latin America to maintain stability.
  • Panama Canal: Increased U.S. military and trade power by connecting the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans.

Topic 7.4 — World War I

⚔️ America Enters a Global War

The United States tried to stay neutral when World War I began in Europe, but German submarine warfare, economic ties to the Allies, and the Zimmermann Telegram pushed the U.S. into the war in 1917.

Reasons for U.S. Entry

  • Unrestricted submarine warfare: Germany attacked ships, including those carrying Americans.
  • Zimmermann Telegram: Germany encouraged Mexico to attack the United States.
  • Economic ties: The U.S. traded heavily with Britain and France.
  • Wilson's ideals: Wilson argued the U.S. was fighting to make the world “safe for democracy.”

Home Front

  • War Industries Board: Coordinated wartime production.
  • Food Administration: Encouraged conservation and increased food production.
  • Great Migration: African Americans moved from the South to Northern cities for wartime jobs.
  • Espionage and Sedition Acts: Restricted speech critical of the war.

After the War

  • Fourteen Points: Wilson's plan for peace, self-determination, and a League of Nations.
  • Senate rejection: The U.S. rejected the Treaty of Versailles and did not join the League of Nations.
  • First Red Scare: Fear of communism and radicalism led to raids and deportations.

Topic 7.5 — The 1920s

🎷 Prosperity, Modern Culture, and Conflict

The 1920s were marked by consumer culture, automobiles, radio, movies, jazz, changing gender roles, and conflicts between modern and traditional values.

Consumer Culture

  • Automobile: Henry Ford's assembly line made cars cheaper and changed work, suburbs, roads, and leisure.
  • Installment buying: Consumers bought goods on credit.
  • Advertising: Businesses used modern marketing to create demand.
  • Mass media: Radio and movies created a shared national culture.

Cultural Change

  • Harlem Renaissance: African American cultural movement featuring writers, musicians, and artists.
  • Jazz Age: Jazz music symbolized modern urban culture.
  • Flappers: Young women who challenged traditional gender expectations.
  • Lost Generation: Writers criticized materialism and disillusionment after World War I.

Cultural Conflict

  • Prohibition: Banned alcohol but increased organized crime and speakeasies.
  • Scopes Trial: Conflict between religious fundamentalism and modern science.
  • Nativism: Immigration restrictions increased, including the National Origins Act.
  • KKK revival: The Klan targeted Black Americans, immigrants, Catholics, Jews, and modern culture.

Topic 7.6 — The Great Depression

📉 Economic Collapse

The Great Depression was the worst economic crisis in U.S. history. It began after the stock market crash of 1929 and led to mass unemployment, bank failures, poverty, and homelessness.

Causes

  • Stock speculation: Many people bought stocks on margin, creating risky investments.
  • Overproduction: Factories and farms produced more than people could buy.
  • Unequal wealth: Many Americans lacked enough income to keep buying goods.
  • Bank failures: Banks collapsed, wiping out savings and reducing credit.
  • Dust Bowl: Drought and poor farming practices devastated the Great Plains.

Hoover's Response

  • Voluntary cooperation: Hoover hoped businesses and charities would help without major federal intervention.
  • Reconstruction Finance Corporation: Gave loans to banks and businesses.
  • Bonus Army: Veterans demanding early bonus payments were removed by force, hurting Hoover's reputation.

Topic 7.7 — The New Deal

🏛️ Federal Government Expands

Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal used the federal government to provide relief, recovery, and reform. It did not end the Great Depression completely, but it changed Americans' expectations of government.

The Three R's

  • Relief: Immediate help for people in need.
  • Recovery: Programs to restart economic growth.
  • Reform: Changes to prevent another depression.

Major Programs

  • FDIC: Insured bank deposits.
  • CCC: Gave young men jobs in conservation projects.
  • AAA: Tried to raise farm prices by reducing production.
  • WPA: Created jobs building roads, schools, parks, and public projects.
  • Social Security Act: Created old-age pensions and unemployment insurance.
  • Wagner Act: Protected workers' rights to unionize and bargain collectively.

Critics

  • Conservatives: Said the New Deal gave the government too much power and spent too much money.
  • Radicals/populists: Said the New Deal did not do enough to redistribute wealth.
  • Huey Long: Proposed a “Share Our Wealth” program.
  • Supreme Court: Struck down some New Deal programs, leading to Roosevelt's court-packing plan.

Topic 7.8 — World War II

🌍 Arsenal of Democracy

The United States moved from neutrality to full involvement in World War II after Japan attacked Pearl Harbor. The war transformed the economy, expanded federal power, and made the U.S. a global superpower.

Road to War

  • Neutrality Acts: Tried to keep the U.S. out of foreign wars during the 1930s.
  • Lend-Lease Act: Allowed the U.S. to supply Allied nations before entering the war.
  • Atlantic Charter: Roosevelt and Churchill outlined goals for the postwar world.
  • Pearl Harbor: Japan's attack on December 7, 1941, brought the U.S. into the war.

Home Front

  • War production: Factories produced weapons, planes, ships, and supplies.
  • Women workers: Rosie the Riveter symbolized women entering wartime industrial jobs.
  • African Americans: The Double V Campaign called for victory against fascism abroad and racism at home.
  • Mexican Americans: The Bracero Program brought Mexican workers to fill labor shortages.
  • Japanese American incarceration: Executive Order 9066 forced many Japanese Americans into camps.

End of the War

  • D-Day: Allied invasion of Normandy began liberation of Western Europe.
  • Island hopping: U.S. strategy to move across the Pacific toward Japan.
  • Atomic bombs: The U.S. dropped atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, leading to Japan's surrender.
  • United Nations: Created after the war to promote international cooperation.

Cross-Regional Continuities & Change

  • Continuity: Americans continued debating the role of government, immigration, race, gender, and foreign policy.
  • Change: The federal government became much more active in regulating the economy and responding to crisis.
  • Similarities: Progressivism, the New Deal, and wartime mobilization all expanded government action.
  • Differences: The U.S. rejected long-term global commitments after World War I but embraced global leadership after World War II.

📝 Multiple Choice Practice

Click an answer to check it. Each question includes an explanation.

1. Which of the following was a major goal of Progressive reformers?

(A) Eliminate all federal regulation of business.
(B) Use government action to address problems caused by industrialization and urbanization.
(C) Restore slavery in the South.
(D) End women's suffrage permanently.
Answer: B. Progressives believed government could solve social, economic, and political problems caused by industrial growth.

2. Which event most directly helped make the United States an overseas imperial power?

(A) The Election of 1896.
(B) The Spanish-American War.
(C) The Scopes Trial.
(D) The Dust Bowl.
Answer: B. After the Spanish-American War, the U.S. gained Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines, becoming an overseas imperial power.

3. Which factor contributed to U.S. entry into World War I?

(A) The attack on Pearl Harbor.
(B) Unrestricted submarine warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram.
(C) The Korean War.
(D) The fall of the Berlin Wall.
Answer: B. German submarine attacks and the Zimmermann Telegram helped push the U.S. into World War I in 1917.

4. Which statement best describes the New Deal?

(A) It completely ended the Great Depression immediately.
(B) It expanded the federal government's role in the economy through relief, recovery, and reform programs.
(C) It ended all labor unions.
(D) It eliminated Social Security.
Answer: B. The New Deal expanded federal responsibility for economic relief, regulation, jobs, banking, labor rights, and social welfare.

5. Executive Order 9066 is most closely associated with which development?

(A) The creation of Social Security.
(B) The Harlem Renaissance.
(C) The incarceration of Japanese Americans during World War II.
(D) The passage of the Pure Food and Drug Act.
Answer: C. Executive Order 9066 authorized the removal and incarceration of many Japanese Americans during World War II.

⭐ Key Takeaways

  • Progressivism expanded reform. Reformers used government action to address corruption, unsafe products, labor problems, and inequality.
  • The U.S. became an imperial power. The Spanish-American War gave the U.S. overseas territories and expanded global influence.
  • World War I increased federal power. The government managed industry, limited speech, and mobilized society for war.
  • The 1920s mixed prosperity and conflict. Consumer culture grew, but nativism, Prohibition, racism, and cultural tensions also increased.
  • The Great Depression transformed politics. Economic collapse led to the New Deal and a larger federal role in Americans' lives.
  • World War II made the U.S. a superpower. Wartime production ended the Depression and positioned the U.S. as a global leader.
  • Rights expanded unevenly. Women gained suffrage, but segregation, Japanese American incarceration, and racial discrimination continued.
