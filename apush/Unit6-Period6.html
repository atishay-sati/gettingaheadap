Unit 6: Period 6

1865 – 1898  |  Exam Weight: 10–17%

🇺🇸 Unit Overview

APUSH Unit 6 covers the Gilded Age, a period of rapid industrial growth, urbanization, immigration, labor conflict, western expansion, and political corruption. Railroads, steel, oil, and banking helped create huge corporations and massive fortunes. At the same time, workers, farmers, immigrants, Native Americans, and urban poor people faced serious challenges.

Big question: How did industrialization, migration, western expansion, and political conflict transform the United States between 1865 and 1898?

Topic 6.1 — Contextualizing Period 6

🏭 The Gilded Age

The Gilded Age refers to the late 1800s, when America looked wealthy and successful on the surface but had serious problems underneath, including poverty, corruption, inequality, racism, and dangerous working conditions.

Major Themes

  • Industrialization: The U.S. became a major industrial power through railroads, steel, oil, coal, and factories.
  • Urbanization: Cities grew quickly as immigrants and rural Americans moved for jobs.
  • Immigration: Millions of immigrants arrived, especially from southern and eastern Europe and Asia.
  • Labor conflict: Workers organized unions to fight long hours, low wages, and unsafe conditions.
  • Western expansion: Settlers, railroads, ranchers, and miners transformed the West and displaced Native peoples.

Topic 6.2 — Industrial Capitalism

🏭 Big Business and Industrial Growth

After the Civil War, the United States industrialized rapidly. New technologies, railroads, natural resources, immigration, and investment helped large corporations grow.

Causes of Industrialization

  • Natural resources: Coal, iron, oil, timber, and land supported industrial growth.
  • Labor supply: Immigrants and rural migrants provided workers for factories and railroads.
  • Technology: Bessemer steel, telegraphs, electricity, and machinery increased production.
  • Railroads: Connected markets, moved goods, and encouraged settlement.
  • Government support: Land grants, subsidies, tariffs, and limited regulation helped businesses grow.

Big Business Leaders

  • Andrew Carnegie: Built a steel empire using vertical integration.
  • John D. Rockefeller: Built Standard Oil using horizontal integration.
  • J. P. Morgan: Powerful banker who helped finance and consolidate corporations.
  • Cornelius Vanderbilt: Built wealth through railroads and shipping.

Business Methods

  • Vertical integration: A company controls every step of production.
  • Horizontal integration: A company buys out competitors in the same industry.
  • Trusts: Business combinations that reduced competition and increased corporate power.
  • Monopoly: A company dominates an entire industry.
Andrew Carnegie — connects to steel, vertical integration, and the rise of big business.

Topic 6.3 — Social and Economic Ideas

💰 Defending and Criticizing Wealth

Industrial growth created extreme wealth for some and poverty for many others. Americans debated whether inequality was natural, moral, or a problem government should fix.

Ideas Supporting Big Business

  • Laissez-faire: Government should not interfere much in the economy.
  • Social Darwinism: Applied “survival of the fittest” to society and defended inequality as natural.
  • Gospel of Wealth: Carnegie argued the rich should use their wealth to benefit society through philanthropy.

Criticism of Big Business

  • Critics called powerful industrialists “robber barons” because they used unfair practices and exploited workers.
  • Farmers argued railroads and banks charged unfair rates.
  • Workers argued corporations paid low wages and created unsafe workplaces.

Topic 6.4 — Labor in the Gilded Age

🛠️ Workers and Unions

Industrial workers often faced long hours, low pay, dangerous conditions, child labor, and little job security. Many formed unions to demand better treatment.

Major Labor Organizations

  • Knights of Labor: Wanted broad reform and included skilled, unskilled, Black, and women workers.
  • American Federation of Labor: Led by Samuel Gompers; focused on skilled workers, higher wages, shorter hours, and better conditions.
  • Socialists and radicals: Some workers wanted major changes to capitalism itself.

Major Strikes

  • Great Railroad Strike of 1877: Federal troops helped suppress workers protesting wage cuts.
  • Haymarket Affair: Bombing during a labor protest hurt public support for unions.
  • Homestead Strike: Violent conflict between Carnegie Steel workers and private security forces.
  • Pullman Strike: Federal government used troops and court orders to crush a railroad strike.

Why Unions Struggled

  • Employers used strikebreakers, blacklists, private police, and lockouts.
  • Courts and the federal government often sided with business owners.
  • Public opinion sometimes turned against unions after violent events.
Haymarket Affair — connects to labor conflict and public fear of radical unions.

Topic 6.5 — Immigration and Urbanization

🏙️ Growing Cities and New Immigrants

Industrial jobs attracted millions of immigrants and rural Americans to cities. Urban growth created opportunity but also overcrowding, poverty, disease, crime, and political corruption.

Immigration

  • Old immigrants: Earlier immigrants often came from northern and western Europe.
  • New immigrants: Later immigrants came more often from southern and eastern Europe, including Italy, Russia, Poland, and the Balkans.
  • Asian immigration: Chinese immigrants worked in railroads, mining, agriculture, and service jobs but faced intense discrimination.
  • Chinese Exclusion Act: 1882 law that banned most Chinese immigration.

Urban Problems

  • Tenements: Crowded apartment buildings where many poor immigrants lived.
  • Political machines: Organizations like Tammany Hall provided services in exchange for votes.
  • Settlement houses: Reformers like Jane Addams created places such as Hull House to help immigrants and the urban poor.
  • Nativism: Hostility toward immigrants increased because of job competition, religion, ethnicity, and racism.
Mulberry Street, New York City — connects to immigration, urbanization, and crowded city life.

Topic 6.6 — The West and Native Americans

🐎 Western Expansion and Native Resistance

The federal government, settlers, railroads, miners, and ranchers transformed the West after the Civil War. This expansion came at the expense of Native Americans, who were forced onto reservations and pressured to assimilate.

Western Settlement

  • Homestead Act: Offered land to settlers willing to farm and improve it.
  • Railroads: Encouraged settlement, trade, migration, and development.
  • Mining: Gold, silver, and other minerals attracted settlers to western boomtowns.
  • Cattle ranching: Cowboys and cattle drives became important before barbed wire and farming changed the open range.

Native American Conflict and Assimilation

  • Reservations: Native peoples were forced onto limited lands controlled by the federal government.
  • Battle of Little Bighorn: Sioux and Cheyenne forces defeated Custer's troops in 1876.
  • Dawes Act: Divided tribal lands into individual plots to force assimilation and weaken tribal identity.
  • Wounded Knee: 1890 massacre of Lakota people that marked the end of major armed Plains resistance.
Wounded Knee aftermath — connects to Native resistance, U.S. military force, and the end of the Plains Indian Wars.

Topic 6.7 — Farmers and Populism

🌾 Farmers Fight Back

Farmers faced falling crop prices, high railroad rates, debt, and tight money. They organized politically through the Grange, Farmers' Alliances, and the Populist Party.

Farmer Problems

  • Debt: Many farmers borrowed money for land, seed, tools, and equipment.
  • Railroad rates: Farmers believed railroads charged unfair prices to ship crops.
  • Deflation: Falling prices made debts harder to repay.
  • Middlemen: Farmers felt exploited by banks, grain elevator operators, and railroad companies.

Populist Goals

  • Free silver: Expand the money supply to help debtors.
  • Railroad regulation: Make shipping rates fairer.
  • Graduated income tax: Tax wealthier people at higher rates.
  • Direct election of senators: Make government more democratic.
  • Government ownership: Some Populists wanted public control of railroads and telegraphs.

Election of 1896

  • William Jennings Bryan: Supported free silver and gave the “Cross of Gold” speech.
  • William McKinley: Supported the gold standard and won with support from business interests.
  • Significance: Populism declined as a party, but many Populist ideas later became reforms.

Topic 6.8 — Politics in the Gilded Age

🏛️ Corruption, Party Machines, and Limited Reform

Gilded Age politics were marked by close elections, party loyalty, patronage, corruption, and limited government action. Political machines controlled many cities, while reformers pushed for civil service reform and regulation.

Political Issues

  • Patronage: Politicians rewarded supporters with government jobs.
  • Political machines: Urban organizations traded services and jobs for votes.
  • Tammany Hall: Famous New York City political machine associated with Boss Tweed.
  • Civil service reform: Pendleton Civil Service Act created merit-based government jobs after Garfield's assassination.

Government Regulation

  • Interstate Commerce Act: Created the ICC to regulate railroads.
  • Sherman Antitrust Act: Tried to restrict monopolies and trusts, but was weakly enforced at first.
  • Laissez-faire limits: Government usually avoided strong regulation of business.

Topic 6.9 — Race and Segregation After Reconstruction

⚖️ Jim Crow and the Loss of Reconstruction Gains

After Reconstruction ended, Southern states created systems of racial segregation and voter suppression. African Americans faced violence, discrimination, and legal inequality.

Disenfranchisement

  • Poll taxes: Fees required to vote.
  • Literacy tests: Tests used to block Black voters.
  • Grandfather clauses: Protected poor white voters while excluding Black voters.

Segregation

  • Jim Crow laws: Laws that enforced racial segregation in public life.
  • Plessy v. Ferguson: Supreme Court upheld “separate but equal” segregation in 1896.
  • Violence: Lynching and white supremacist terror were used to enforce racial control.

  • Differences: Industrialists gained wealth and power, while workers and farmers often struggled against corporations.

📝 Multiple Choice Practice

Click an answer to check it. Each question includes an explanation.

1. Which factor most directly encouraged rapid industrial growth after the Civil War?

(A) The end of railroad construction.
(B) Natural resources, railroads, technology, labor, and government support.
(C) The disappearance of corporations.
(D) The ban on immigration.
Answer: B. Industrial growth depended on resources, transportation, inventions, workers, capital, and government support.

2. What was the main goal of the American Federation of Labor?

(A) Immediate government ownership of all farms.
(B) Better wages, shorter hours, and improved conditions for skilled workers.
(C) Ending all immigration from Europe.
(D) Returning Native lands to tribal control.
Answer: B. The AFL focused on practical workplace goals for skilled workers, especially wages, hours, and conditions.

3. Which law attempted to assimilate Native Americans by dividing tribal lands into individual plots?

(A) Chinese Exclusion Act.
(B) Dawes Act.
(C) Sherman Antitrust Act.
(D) Pendleton Civil Service Act.
Answer: B. The Dawes Act divided tribal lands into individual plots to weaken tribal identity and force assimilation.

4. Which group most strongly supported free silver in the 1890s?

(A) Large eastern bankers.
(B) Farmers and Populists who wanted inflation to make debts easier to pay.
(C) Railroad executives who wanted higher shipping rates.
(D) Southern segregationists who supported poll taxes.
Answer: B. Farmers and Populists supported free silver because inflation would raise crop prices and make debts easier to repay.

5. Plessy v. Ferguson is most closely associated with which development?

(A) The end of industrial capitalism.
(B) The creation of the Populist Party.
(C) The constitutional approval of racial segregation under “separate but equal.”
(D) The expansion of Chinese immigration.
Answer: C. Plessy v. Ferguson upheld segregation and allowed Jim Crow laws to continue under the idea of “separate but equal.”

⭐ Key Takeaways

  • The U.S. became an industrial giant. Railroads, steel, oil, factories, and finance transformed the economy.
  • Big business gained huge power. Carnegie, Rockefeller, Morgan, and Vanderbilt built corporations that dominated industries.
  • Workers organized but struggled. Unions fought for better conditions, but government and courts often sided with business.
  • Cities grew quickly. Immigration and industrial jobs caused rapid urbanization, tenements, political machines, and reform efforts.
  • Native peoples lost land and sovereignty. Federal policy pushed reservations, assimilation, and military force.
  • Farmers organized through Populism. They demanded railroad regulation, free silver, income tax, and more democracy.
  • Segregation expanded after Reconstruction. Jim Crow laws, disenfranchisement, and Plessy v. Ferguson limited Black rights.
