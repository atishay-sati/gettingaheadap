🇺🇸 Unit Overview

APUSH Unit 9 covers the United States from the rise of modern conservatism in the 1980s to the present. This period includes Reagan conservatism, the end of the Cold War, globalization, technological change, immigration, terrorism, wars in the Middle East, political polarization, economic inequality, and major social debates.

Big question: How did conservatism, globalization, technology, terrorism, immigration, and political division reshape the United States from 1980 to the present?

Topic 9.1 — Contextualizing Period 9

🌎 America in a Globalized World

After 1980, the United States became more conservative politically, more global economically, and more divided culturally. The end of the Cold War left the U.S. as the world's strongest superpower, but new challenges emerged, including terrorism, globalization, immigration debates, economic inequality, and political polarization.

Major Themes

  • Conservatism: Many Americans supported lower taxes, smaller government, deregulation, stronger military spending, and traditional social values.
  • Globalization: Trade, technology, and multinational corporations connected the U.S. economy more closely to the world.
  • Technology: Computers, the internet, social media, and automation transformed work, politics, and culture.
  • Immigration: New immigration from Latin America, Asia, Africa, and the Middle East changed American demographics.
  • Security: After 9/11, terrorism became a central focus of U.S. foreign and domestic policy.
  • Polarization: Political parties and voters became more ideologically divided.

Topic 9.2 — Reagan and Modern Conservatism

🦅 Conservative Resurgence

Ronald Reagan's election in 1980 marked a major conservative shift. Reagan argued that government was too large, taxes were too high, and the U.S. needed a stronger military and more free-market policies.

Reaganomics

  • Supply-side economics: Belief that cutting taxes and regulations would encourage investment, business growth, and job creation.
  • Tax cuts: Reagan lowered income taxes, especially for higher earners and businesses.
  • Deregulation: Reduced government rules on business and industry.
  • Military spending: Reagan increased defense spending to pressure the Soviet Union.
  • National debt: Tax cuts and military spending contributed to rising deficits.

Conservative Coalition

  • Economic conservatives: Wanted lower taxes, less regulation, and free-market policies.
  • Social conservatives: Opposed abortion, supported traditional family values, and promoted religion in public life.
  • Neoconservatives: Supported a strong military and aggressive foreign policy against communism.
  • Sun Belt voters: Growth in the South and West helped strengthen conservatism.

Topic 9.3 — The End of the Cold War

🧊 Collapse of the Soviet Union

During the 1980s and early 1990s, the Cold War came to an end. Economic problems in the Soviet Union, reforms by Gorbachev, pressure from the U.S., and democratic movements in Eastern Europe weakened communist governments.

Causes

  • Gorbachev's reforms: Glasnost allowed more openness; perestroika attempted economic restructuring.
  • Economic weakness: The Soviet economy struggled to keep up with military spending and consumer needs.
  • Eastern Europe: Democratic movements challenged Soviet-backed governments.
  • U.S. pressure: Reagan's military buildup and anti-communist rhetoric increased pressure on the Soviet Union.

Effects

  • Berlin Wall falls: In 1989, the symbol of Cold War division collapsed.
  • Soviet Union dissolves: In 1991, the USSR broke apart into independent republics.
  • U.S. superpower status: The U.S. became the world's dominant military and economic power.
  • New foreign policy questions: Without the Soviet Union, the U.S. faced new debates over intervention, terrorism, and global leadership.
Fall of the Berlin Wall — connects to the end of the Cold War and collapse of communist control in Eastern Europe.

Topic 9.4 — Globalization and the New Economy

🌐 Trade, Outsourcing, and Economic Change

Globalization connected the U.S. economy more closely to other countries through trade, finance, technology, and multinational corporations. It created new opportunities but also caused job loss and wage pressure in some industries.

Major Developments

  • NAFTA: Increased trade between the U.S., Mexico, and Canada.
  • Outsourcing: Companies moved some jobs overseas to reduce labor costs.
  • Deindustrialization: Manufacturing jobs declined in many older industrial regions.
  • Service economy: More jobs shifted toward health care, education, finance, technology, and retail.
  • Income inequality: Wealth gaps widened as high-skill and finance/technology jobs grew faster than many working-class jobs.

Debates Over Globalization

  • Supporters argued free trade lowered prices, expanded markets, and encouraged economic growth.
  • Critics argued globalization hurt American workers, weakened unions, and moved jobs overseas.
  • Trade became a major political issue in debates over economic inequality and national identity.

Topic 9.5 — Technology and Cultural Change

💻 Digital Revolution

Computers, the internet, smartphones, and social media transformed American society. Technology changed the economy, education, communication, entertainment, politics, and privacy.

Technology Changes

  • Personal computers: Became common in homes, schools, and workplaces.
  • Internet: Connected people to information, communication, shopping, and global networks.
  • Social media: Changed politics, activism, news, identity, and public debate.
  • Automation: Increased productivity but also replaced some jobs.
  • Digital divide: Unequal access to technology created new inequalities.

Cultural Impact

  • Entertainment became more global and more personalized.
  • Political campaigns used television, internet organizing, and social media.
  • Online communication changed how Americans formed communities and debated issues.
  • Privacy, misinformation, and surveillance became major concerns.
Early personal computer — connects to the digital revolution and the technology-driven economy.

Topic 9.6 — Immigration and Demographic Change

🧳 A More Diverse America

Immigration after 1965 continued to reshape the United States. More immigrants came from Latin America, Asia, Africa, and the Middle East, making the country more diverse and increasing debates over immigration policy.

Causes of Immigration

  • 1965 Immigration Act: Ended national origins quotas and opened immigration to more regions.
  • Economic opportunity: Many immigrants came for jobs, education, and better living conditions.
  • Refugees: Wars and political conflict brought refugees from Vietnam, Cuba, Central America, the Middle East, and elsewhere.
  • Family reunification: Immigration law allowed many families to reconnect in the United States.

Effects

  • The U.S. became more racially, ethnically, religiously, and linguistically diverse.
  • Immigrants shaped cities, suburbs, schools, labor markets, food, culture, and politics.
  • Debates grew over border security, citizenship, undocumented immigration, and multiculturalism.

Topic 9.7 — Terrorism, Security, and the War on Terror

🛡️ September 11 and U.S. Foreign Policy

The September 11, 2001 attacks changed U.S. foreign and domestic policy. The federal government expanded national security powers and launched wars in Afghanistan and Iraq.

9/11 and Immediate Effects

  • Al-Qaeda: Terrorist group responsible for the September 11 attacks.
  • War in Afghanistan: The U.S. invaded Afghanistan to target al-Qaeda and the Taliban.
  • Department of Homeland Security: Created to coordinate domestic security.
  • USA PATRIOT Act: Expanded surveillance and law enforcement powers to fight terrorism.

Iraq War

  • 2003 invasion: The U.S. invaded Iraq, arguing Saddam Hussein had weapons of mass destruction.
  • Controversy: No active WMD program was found, damaging trust in government claims.
  • Long-term effects: The war caused major casualties, instability, debt, and debate over U.S. intervention.
September 11 attacks — connects to the War on Terror, homeland security, and foreign policy after 2001.

Topic 9.8 — Modern Politics and Polarization

🗳️ Polarized America

Since the 1980s, American politics has become more polarized. Conservatives and liberals have sharply disagreed over taxes, regulation, abortion, gun rights, immigration, health care, climate policy, race, gender, and the role of government.

Major Political Developments

  • Reagan Revolution: Strengthened modern conservatism and reduced trust in large federal government programs.
  • Clinton era: Democrats moved toward the political center on welfare reform, crime, and free trade.
  • Contract with America: Republicans gained Congress in 1994 with promises of smaller government and conservative reform.
  • Bush v. Gore: Supreme Court decision helped decide the disputed 2000 election.
  • Obama presidency: Expanded health insurance through the Affordable Care Act and represented a major racial milestone.
  • Trump era: Intensified debates over immigration, nationalism, trade, media, courts, and executive power.

Modern Social Issues

  • Abortion: Continued as a major political and legal conflict.
  • Gun rights: Debates over the Second Amendment and mass shootings intensified.
  • LGBTQ+ rights: Activism led to expanding legal recognition, including same-sex marriage.
  • Climate change: Environmental policy became a major partisan issue.
  • Racial justice: Movements challenged police violence, inequality, and systemic racism.

  • Continuity: Americans continued debating federal power, economic inequality, immigration, civil rights, and global involvement.

📝 Multiple Choice Practice

Click an answer to check it. Each question includes an explanation.

1. Which of the following best describes Reaganomics?

(A) Expanding New Deal-style welfare programs and raising taxes.
(B) Cutting taxes, reducing regulation, and supporting supply-side economics.
(C) Ending military spending and withdrawing from the Cold War.
(D) Nationalizing major industries under federal control.
Answer: B. Reaganomics focused on tax cuts, deregulation, reduced domestic spending, and the belief that business growth would benefit the wider economy.

2. Which event symbolized the collapse of communist control in Eastern Europe?

(A) The creation of NAFTA.
(B) The fall of the Berlin Wall.
(C) The passage of the PATRIOT Act.
(D) The start of the Iraq War.
Answer: B. The fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 symbolized the weakening of Soviet control in Eastern Europe and the approaching end of the Cold War.

3. Which of the following was a major criticism of globalization?

(A) It completely ended international trade.
(B) It encouraged outsourcing and contributed to manufacturing job loss in some communities.
(C) It prevented companies from moving goods across borders.
(D) It ended economic inequality.
Answer: B. Critics argued globalization moved jobs overseas, weakened unions, and hurt some industrial workers and communities.

4. The PATRIOT Act was passed mainly in response to which event?

(A) The fall of the Soviet Union.
(B) The September 11 terrorist attacks.
(C) The election of 1980.
(D) The creation of the internet.
Answer: B. The PATRIOT Act was passed after 9/11 and expanded federal surveillance and law enforcement powers to fight terrorism.

5. Which statement best describes political polarization in modern America?

(A) Political parties became nearly identical on all major issues.
(B) Political parties and voters became more ideologically divided.
(C) Americans stopped debating social issues.
(D) Federal elections ended after 2000.
Answer: B. Political polarization means Democrats and Republicans, as well as their voters, became more divided over ideology, culture, and policy.

⭐ Key Takeaways

  • Conservatism rose after 1980. Reagan promoted tax cuts, deregulation, smaller domestic government, stronger military spending, and traditional values.
  • The Cold War ended. The Soviet Union collapsed, leaving the U.S. as the world's leading superpower.
  • Globalization reshaped the economy. Free trade, outsourcing, finance, and multinational corporations changed work and widened some inequalities.
  • Technology transformed daily life. Computers, the internet, smartphones, and social media changed communication, politics, and the economy.
  • Immigration changed American demographics. Post-1965 immigration made the United States more diverse and fueled political debates.
  • 9/11 changed national security. The War on Terror, PATRIOT Act, Afghanistan, and Iraq reshaped foreign and domestic policy.
  • Political polarization increased. Americans became more divided over government, culture, race, immigration, health care, climate, and social issues.
