The 9 Units

The College Board organizes AP World History: Modern into nine units across four time periods. Approximate exam weighting is shown on each card.

UNIT 1

The Global Tapestry

c. 1200 – c. 1450
Compare state-building in Song China, the Islamic world (Abbasid fragmentation, Delhi Sultanate), South/Southeast Asia, the Americas (Aztec, Inca), Africa, and feudal Europe.
Exam weight: 8–10%
UNIT 2

Networks of Exchange

c. 1200 – c. 1450
Silk Roads, Indian Ocean, and Trans-Saharan trade; the Mongol Empire; the spread of Islam, Buddhism, and disease (Black Death) across Afro-Eurasia.
Exam weight: 8–10%
UNIT 3

Land-Based Empires

c. 1450 – c. 1750
The "gunpowder empires" — Ottoman, Safavid, Mughal — plus Ming/Qing China and Russia. How rulers consolidated and legitimized power.
Exam weight: 12–15%
UNIT 4

Transoceanic Interconnections

c. 1450 – c. 1750
European maritime expansion, the Columbian Exchange, the Atlantic slave trade, mercantilism, and the rise of global silver and sugar economies.
Exam weight: 12–15%
UNIT 5

Revolutions

c. 1750 – c. 1900
Enlightenment thought; American, French, Haitian, and Latin American revolutions; the Industrial Revolution; nationalism; and new economic ideologies (capitalism, Marxism).
Exam weight: 12–15%
UNIT 6

Consequences of Industrialization

c. 1750 – c. 1900
New imperialism, the Scramble for Africa, economic imperialism in Asia, global migration, and anti-imperial resistance movements.
Exam weight: 12–15%
UNIT 7

Global Conflict

c. 1900 – Present
WWI and WWII, the interwar period, totalitarian regimes, the Russian Revolution, the Holocaust, and the collapse of empires.
Exam weight: 8–10%
UNIT 8

Cold War and Decolonization

c. 1900 – Present
The US–USSR rivalry, proxy wars, Chinese Communist Revolution, independence movements in Africa and Asia, and non-aligned nations.
Exam weight: 8–10%
UNIT 9

Globalization

c. 1900 – Present
Technological change, environmental issues, economic globalization, human rights movements, terrorism, and the post-Cold War order.
Exam weight: 8–10%

The 6 Course Themes

Every unit connects back to these themes. Mastering them is the secret to strong LEQ and DBQ responses.

ENV

Humans & the Environment

How geography, climate, disease, and resources shape — and are shaped by — human societies.

CDI

Cultural Developments & Interactions

Religion, philosophy, science, and art — and how ideas spread, blend, and clash across cultures.

GOV

Governance

How states form, consolidate, legitimize, expand, and decline — from empires to nation-states.

ECN

Economic Systems

Agriculture, trade networks, labor systems, industrialization, capitalism, and globalization.

SOC

Social Interactions & Organization

Class, race, gender, family, and ethnicity — how societies organize people and hierarchies.

TEC

Technology & Innovation

Tools, weapons, and ideas — from the printing press and gunpowder to the Internet.

The AP Exam

3 hours and 15 minutes total. Section I = 60% of your score. Section II = 40%.

Multiple Choice

55 questions · 55 minutes · 40% of score
  • • Stimulus-based sets of 3–4 questions
  • • Primary/secondary sources, maps, charts
  • • Tests reasoning, not memorization

Short Answer (SAQ)

3 questions · 40 minutes · 20% of score
  • • Q1: Secondary source (required)
  • • Q2: Primary source (required)
  • • Q3 or Q4: Choose one (no stimulus)
  • • Answer in A/B/C parts — no thesis needed

Document-Based Question (DBQ)

1 question · 60 min (incl. 15 min read) · 25% of score
  • • 7 documents to analyze
  • • Thesis + contextualization
  • • Use 6 docs + sourcing on 3
  • • 1 piece of outside evidence
  • • Complex understanding

Long Essay (LEQ)

1 question · 40 minutes · 15% of score
  • • Choose 1 of 3 prompts (different eras)
  • • Thesis + contextualization
  • • 2 pieces of specific evidence
  • • Historical reasoning (compare, cause, CCOT)
  • • Complex understanding

Historical Reasoning Skills

AP World tests how you think about history, not just what you remember. These are the moves you need to practice until they're automatic.

Comparison

Identify similarities and differences between historical developments, societies, or processes — and explain why those differences matter.

Causation

Distinguish causes from effects, differentiate long-term from short-term causes, and weigh which factors mattered most.

Continuity & Change Over Time

Recognize what persists across eras and what shifts — a core skill for LEQ prompts that span centuries.

Contextualization

Situate a specific event inside broader regional or global developments happening before or around it.

Sourcing & Situation (HIPP)

For every document, analyze Historical context, Intended audience, Purpose, and author's Point of view.

Argumentation

Build a defensible, historically specific thesis and support it with evidence — the heartbeat of every DBQ and LEQ.
